id: fc-peptide-design-stapling
front: "What are stapled peptides and why are they used?"
back: "Stapled peptides have their alpha-helical conformation locked by a covalent cross-link (hydrocarbon, lactam, or disulfide staple) between side chains. This improves: 1) binding affinity by reducing conformational entropy, 2) proteolytic stability, 3) cell permeability in some cases. Example: ALRN-6924 (MDM2/MDMX inhibitor)."

id: fc-peptide-design-protac
front: "What are peptide-based PROTACs?"
back: "PROTACs (Proteolysis Targeting Chimeras) are bifunctional molecules with a peptide ligand for an E3 ubiquitin ligase connected to a target-binding moiety. They recruit the proteasome to degrade target proteins catalytically. Peptide PROTACs use phage-display-derived binders for E3 ligases like VHL or cereblon."

id: fc-peptide-design-damino
front: "How do D-amino acids improve peptide drug properties?"
back: "D-amino acids resist L-specific proteases, dramatically increasing metabolic stability. They can maintain binding if the residue is not involved in direct receptor contact. Fully retro-inverso peptides (all D-amino acids, reversed sequence) mimic the side chain topology of the L-peptide. Caveat: immunogenicity may increase."

front: "What are cell-penetrating peptides (CPPs) and what are common examples?"
back: "CPPs are short peptides (5-30 residues) that facilitate cellular uptake of cargo. Examples: TAT (YGRKKRRQRRR), penetratin (RQIKIWFQNRRMKWKK), polyarginine (R8-R12). They enter via endocytosis and may require endosomal escape strategies. Cationic charge and amphipathicity are key features."

id: fc-peptide-delivery-endosomal
front: "What is the endosomal escape problem in peptide delivery?"
back: "Most CPPs and nanoparticles enter cells via endocytosis but become trapped in endosomes/lysosomes where they are degraded. Only 1-5% typically escapes to cytoplasm. Solutions: pH-sensitive fusogenic peptides (GALA, HA2), chloroquine, photochemical internalization, or proton sponge polymers."

id: fc-peptide-stability-deamidation
front: "What is peptide deamidation and which residues are affected?"
back: "Deamidation is the spontaneous conversion of Asn (or Gln) to Asp (or Glu) + isoAsp via a succinimide intermediate. Asn-Gly and Asn-Ser sequences are most susceptible. It causes charge change, conformational shift, and loss of activity. Rate increases with pH >6, temperature, and buffer type. Prevented by: low pH, low temperature, sequence engineering."
tags: ["stability", "deamidation", "asparagine", "isoaspartate"]

id: fc-peptide-stability-racemization
front: "What is racemization in peptide chemistry?"
back: "Racemization is the conversion of L-amino acids to D-amino acids during synthesis or storage, particularly at Cys, His, and activated residues. It generates diastereomers that may have altered biological activity, receptor binding, and immunogenicity. Monitored by chiral HPLC or Marfey's analysis."

id: fc-peptide-analysis-sequence
front: "What is the difference between bottom-up and top-down proteomics for peptide analysis?"
back: "Bottom-up: enzymatic digestion (trypsin) of protein → LC-MS/MS of peptides → database search. Most common, robust. Top-down: intact protein MS/MS → fragment in instrument. Preserves post-translational modification combinatorics. Middle-down: limited proteolysis of large proteins into medium-sized peptides."

id: fc-peptide-analysis-impurity
front: "What are common process-related impurities in synthetic peptides?"
back: "1) Deletion sequences (missing amino acid). 2) Truncated sequences (incomplete coupling). 3) Deamidation products. 4) Oxidized variants. 5) Diketopiperazine formation. 6) Racemized residues. 7) Residual solvents and reagents (DCM, TFA, piperidine). 8) Counterion content. All require analytical control per ICH guidelines."

id: fc-peptide-analysis-counterion
front: "Why does counterion identity matter for peptide drugs?"
back: "Counterions (TFA, acetate, chloride, trifluoroacetate) affect: 1) Hygroscopicity and solid-state stability. 2) Solubility. 3) Injection site pain (TFA is irritative). 4) Toxicity. 5) Manufacturing reproducibility. Regulatory agencies often require TFA-to-acetate exchange for injectable products. Measured by ion chromatography."

id: fc-peptide-regulation-ichq6
front: "What is ICH Q6A and how does it apply to peptides?"
back: "ICH Q6A defines specifications for chemical drug substances and products. For peptides, it covers: identity tests (amino acid analysis, MS, sequence), purity (RP-HPLC, SE-HPLC), impurity limits, potency (bioassay or HPLC), and stability testing. Provides decision trees for setting acceptance criteria."

id: fc-peptide-design-sar
front: "What is structure-activity relationship (SAR) in peptide drug design?"
back: "SAR maps how sequence/structural changes affect biological activity. Systematic approaches: alanine scanning (replace each residue with Ala to identify critical contacts), truncation studies, D-amino acid scanning, N-methylation scan, and side chain modifications. Enables identification of pharmacophore and development of minimal active peptides."

id: fc-peptide-stability-photodegradation
front: "How does light exposure degrade peptides?"
back: "UV light causes: 1) Tryptophan photooxidation to N-formylkynurenine and kynurenine. 2) Disulfide bond photolysis. 3) Histidine photooxidation. 4) Tyrosine dityrosine cross-linking. Prevention: amber vials, light-protective packaging, antioxidants. Stability studies (ICH Q1B) include photostability testing."

id: fc-peptide-regulation-combination
front: "What regulatory considerations apply to peptide-drug conjugates (PDCs)?"
back: "PDCs combine a peptide targeting moiety with a cytotoxic payload via a linker. They fall under combination product regulations. Key requirements: 1) Characterization of each component. 2) Linker stability/payload release kinetics. 3) Conjugation site control. 4) Dual mechanism of action documentation. May follow either drug or biologic pathway depending on peptide origin."
