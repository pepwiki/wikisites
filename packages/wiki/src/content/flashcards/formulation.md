id: fc-peptide-stability
front: "What are the main factors affecting peptide stability in formulations?"
back: "Peptide stability is influenced by: (1) pH - affects ionization state and degradation rates, (2) temperature - accelerates degradation kinetics, (3) oxidation - from dissolved oxygen or metal ions, (4) hydrolysis - peptide bond cleavage, (5) aggregation - self-association, (6) light exposure, (7) ionic strength, (8) mechanical stress (shear). The relative importance depends on the specific peptide sequence and formulation conditions."

id: fc-ph-effects
front: "How does pH affect peptide formulation stability?"
back: "pH affects: (1) Ionization state of amino acids (Asp, Glu, His, Lys, Arg, Cys), altering charge-charge interactions, (2) Degradation rates - deamidation is fastest at pH 7-9, hydrolysis at extreme pH, (3) Solubility - minimum near isoelectric point, (4) Aggregation propensity - charge screening at low ionic strength, (5) Conformational stability. Most peptides are formulated at pH 4-7 to balance these factors."

id: fc-temperature-effects
front: "How does temperature impact peptide degradation and what is the Arrhenius relationship?"
back: "Temperature accelerates chemical degradation following the Arrhenius equation: k = A·e^(-Ea/RT), where k is rate constant, Ea is activation energy. Higher temperatures increase: hydrolysis, deamidation, oxidation, aggregation rates. Stability testing uses 4°C (long-term), 25°C/60%RH (intermediate), and 40°C/75%RH (accelerated). Peptides typically have Ea of 50-100 kJ/mol, meaning 2-4x faster degradation per 10°C increase."
tags: ["temperature", "Arrhenius", "stability"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-oxidative-degradation
front: "What causes oxidative degradation in peptides and how can it be prevented?"
back: "Oxidation targets Met, Cys, Trp, Tyr, His residues. Causes: dissolved oxygen, metal ion catalysis (Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺), peroxide impurities, light exposure. Prevention strategies: (1) Chelators (EDTA, DTPA) to sequester metal ions, (2) Antioxidants (ascorbic acid, tocopherols), (3) Nitrogen overlay/blanketing, (4) Oxygen scavengers, (5) Protection from light, (6) Low temperature storage."

id: fc-hydrolytic-degradation
front: "What are the mechanisms of hydrolytic degradation in peptides?"
back: "Hydrolytic degradation involves: (1) Direct hydrolysis - nucleophilic attack by water on peptide bonds, (2) Acid/base-catalyzed hydrolysis - accelerated at extreme pH, (3) Specific sequences susceptible to cleavage - Asp-Pro, Asp-Gly, Asn-Gly, (4) Beta-elimination from Cys, Ser, Thr residues. Rate depends on pH (minimum around pH 5), temperature, and neighboring group effects. Formulation at optimal pH and low temperature minimizes hydrolysis."

id: fc-deamidation
front: "What is deamidation and which residues are most susceptible?"
back: "Deamidation converts Asn→Asp/isoAsp and Gln→Glu via succinimide intermediate. Asn is most susceptible, especially in Asn-Gly, Asn-Ser, Asn-His sequences. Rate: fastest at pH 7-9, slower at pH 5-6. Consequences: altered charge, conformation, bioactivity, immunogenicity. Prevention: (1) Formulate at pH 5-6, (2) Low temperature, (3) Avoid susceptible sequences via mutagenesis, (4) Lyophilization."
tags: ["deamidation", "Asn", "isoAsp"]

id: fc-aggregation
front: "What mechanisms drive peptide aggregation and what are the consequences?"
back: "Aggregation mechanisms: (1) Nucleation-dependent polymerization, (2) Surface-induced aggregation, (3) Agitation-induced, (4) Freeze-concentration during freezing. Types: soluble oligomers, sub-visible particles, visible particles. Consequences: reduced potency, altered pharmacokinetics, immunogenicity (anti-drug antibodies), injection site reactions. Prevention: surfactants (polysorbate), optimal pH, co-solvents, low concentration, avoid agitation."

id: fc-fibrillation
front: "What is peptide fibrillation and how does it differ from amorphous aggregation?"
back: "Fibrillation forms highly ordered, cross-beta-sheet amyloid fibrils. Unlike amorphous aggregation: (1) Nucleation-dependent with characteristic lag phase, (2) Fibrils are thermodynamically stable, (3) Seeds accelerate formation, (4) Resistant to denaturants. Factors promoting fibrillation: low pH (near pI), agitation, interfaces, elevated temperature. Prevention: surfactants, co-solvents (sucrose, arginine), avoid mechanical stress, control pH away from pI."
tags: ["fibrillation", "amyloid", "beta-sheet"]

id: fc-lyophilization
front: "What are the critical process parameters in peptide lyophilization?"
back: "Lyophilization (freeze-drying) stages: (1) Freezing - crystallize water, form amorphous matrix (critical: cooling rate, final temperature), (2) Primary drying - sublimation under vacuum (critical: shelf temperature, chamber pressure), (3) Secondary drying - desorption of bound water (critical: temperature, time). Key parameters: Tg' (glass transition of frozen concentrate), residual moisture (<1-3%), cake appearance, reconstitution time. Protectants: sucrose, trehalose replace H-bonds."
tags: ["lyophilization", "freeze-drying", "Tg"]

id: fc-reconstitution
front: "What factors affect reconstitution of lyophilized peptides?"
back: "Reconstitution depends on: (1) Cake porosity and surface area, (2) Hydrophobicity of peptide, (3) Diluent choice (WFI, bacteriostatic water, saline), (4) Diluent temperature (room temp preferred), (5) Volume of diluent, (6) Agitation method (gentle swirling, avoid vigorous shaking). Poor reconstitution may indicate: high protein concentration, incomplete drying, surface denaturation, or aggregation during processing. Reconstitution time should be <2 minutes for most products."

id: fc-buffer-selection
front: "What buffers are commonly used in peptide formulations and why?"
back: "Common buffers: (1) Acetate (pH 4-5.5) - most common, low metal content, (2) Phosphate (pH 6-8) - physiological, but can catalyze oxidation, (3) Citrate (pH 3-6) - chelates metals, (4) Histidine (pH 5.5-6.5) - low ionic strength, (5) Succinate (pH 4-6). Selection criteria: pKa near target pH, ionic strength effects, metal content, compatibility with container closure, stability during storage. Avoid Tris (reacts with peptides) and phosphate (promotes oxidation)."

Must consider: compatibility, regulatory status (GRAS/Ph.Eur.), stability, route of administration."

id: fc-surfactants
front: "How do surfactants protect peptides from aggregation and what types are used?"
back: "Surfactants protect by: (1) Competing for air-liquid interfaces (prevent surface adsorption), (2) Coating hydrophobic surfaces, (3) Disrupting aggregation nuclei, (4) Reducing interfacial tension. Common types: Polysorbate 20 (Tween 20) - preferred for sensitive peptides, Polysorbate 80 (Tween 80) - more hydrophobic, Pluronic F68 - non-ionic block copolymer. Concentration: typically 0.001-0.1%. Issues: oxidation of polysorbates, peroxide formation, sub-visible particles from degraded surfactant."

id: fc-tonicity-agents
front: "What are tonicity agents and how do they affect peptide formulations?"
back: "Tonicity agents adjust osmolality to match physiological conditions (~290 mOsm/kg). Common agents: (1) NaCl - ionic, may affect peptide charge interactions, (2) Mannitol - non-ionic, may crystallize in lyophilized cakes, (3) Sucrose - non-ionic, also stabilizes structure, (4) Glycerol - liquid formulations. Considerations: effect on peptide solubility, stability, aggregation, viscosity. Isotonicity prevents pain on injection, hemolysis, and tissue damage. Ideal range: 280-320 mOsm/kg."

id: fc-extractables
front: "What are extractables and how are they evaluated in peptide container closure systems?"
back: "Extractables are compounds that migrate from packaging materials under exaggerated conditions (elevated temperature, solvents). Sources: rubber stoppers (vulcanizing agents, antioxidants, plasticizers), glass (metal ions, SiO₂), adhesives, coatings. Testing: expose materials to model solvents at stressed conditions, analyze by GC-MS, LC-MS, ICP-MS. Significance: potential toxicity, catalysis of degradation, altered product quality. Characterization guides leachable risk assessment."
tags: ["extractables", "packaging", "safety"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-leachables
front: "What are leachables and how do they differ from extractables?"
back: "Leachables are compounds that migrate into the drug product under normal storage conditions - a subset of extractables. Differences: (1) Extractables - exaggerated conditions, (2) Leachables - actual storage conditions. Sources: rubber stopper components (2-mercaptobenzothiazole, BHT, ZDEC), glass (Al, B, Ba), silicone oil. Impact: potency loss (catalysis), toxicity, altered stability, precipitation. Monitored during stability studies by ICH Q1A/Q1B guidelines."

id: fc-shelf-life
front: "How is shelf life determined for peptide drug products?"
back: "Shelf life (expiry) determined by: (1) Long-term stability data at recommended storage (25°C/60%RH or 5°C), (2) Statistical analysis of degradation rate (linear regression, 95% confidence interval), (3) Time to reach specification limits for potency, purity, degradation products. ICH Q1E requires: minimum 12 months long-term data, acceptable to extrapolate if data shows ≤5% change. Shelf life = time when lower 95% confidence limit meets specification."
tags: ["shelf-life", "ICH-Q1E", "stability"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-accelerated-testing
front: "What is accelerated stability testing and what conditions are used?"
back: "Accelerated testing predicts long-term stability using stressed conditions: 40°C ± 2°C / 75% RH ± 5% for 6 months. Purposes: (1) Identify degradation pathways, (2) Support shelf life extrapolation, (3) Detect formulation vulnerabilities, (4) Shipping condition validation. Additional stress testing: 50°C, freeze-thaw, light (ICH Q1B), agitation. Data used with Arrhenius kinetics to predict room temperature stability. Required by ICH Q1A for registration."

id: fc-glass-vials
front: "What types of glass are used for peptide vials and what are the delamination risks?"
back: "Glass types: (1) Type I borosilicate - most common, low alkali leaching, (2) Type I treated (e.g., Coating Plus) - enhanced chemical resistance, (3) Type II soda-lime - treated with sulfur, less resistant. Delamination: formation of glass flakes/silica lamellae. Risk factors: high pH (>8), phosphate/acetate buffers, high temperature, long storage, autoclaving. Prevention: use Type I glass, optimize washing/pre-treatment, avoid high pH, shorter storage at elevated temperature."

id: fc-implantable-devices
front: "What are the challenges of implantable devices for peptide delivery?"
back: "Implantable delivery challenges: (1) Biocompatibility - foreign body response, fibrosis, (2) Long-term stability of peptide in vivo, (3) Controlled release kinetics - zero-order preferred, (4) Drug loading limitations, (5) Sterilization methods compatible with peptide, (6) Retrieval/removal for refillable systems, (7) Manufacturing complexity and cost. Types: non-degradable (Duros), biodegradable (PLGA rods), in situ forming implants. Applications: hormone therapy (leuprolide), opioid dependence."

id: fc-microparticles
front: "How are PLGA microparticles used for sustained peptide delivery?"
back: "PLGA microparticles (1-100 μm) provide sustained release over weeks-months. Manufacturing: (1) Emulsion-solvent evaporation (W/O/W for hydrophilic peptides), (2) Spray drying, (3) Coacervation. Release mechanisms: diffusion through matrix, erosion of polymer, initial burst from surface-located drug. Challenges: peptide stability during encapsulation (organic solvents, shear), batch-to-batch variability, sterilization (gamma radiation degrades PLGA). Examples: Lupron Depot (leuprolide), Risperdal Consta (risperidone - not peptide but similar technology)."

id: fc-liposomes

back: "Liposomes are spherical vesicles (50 nm - 5 μm) with phospholipid bilayer. Types: (1) Small unilamellar (SUV), (2) Large unilamellar (LUV), (3) Multilamellar (MLV). id: fc-hydrogels
front: "What are hydrogels and how are they applied to peptide delivery?"
back: "Hydrogels are cross-linked polymer networks that swell in water (70-99% water). Types: (1) Physical (reversible) - ionic, H-bonding, (2) Chemical (permanent) - covalent crosslinks, (3) Stimuli-responsive - pH, temperature, enzyme. For peptides: localized delivery, sustained release, injectable in situ gelling (e.g., PEG-based, alginate, hyaluronic acid). Applications: wound healing, intra-articular, tumor-localized. Advantages: minimally invasive, tunable properties. Challenges: burst release, mechanical strength, biodegradation rate."
