back: "High Performance Liquid Chromatography separates compounds based on their differential interactions with a stationary phase (column packing) and a mobile phase (solvent). Components elute at different times based on their chemical properties."
tags: ["HPLC", "chromatography", "separation"]

id: fc-reverse-phase-hplc
front: "What is reverse-phase HPLC and why is it preferred for peptide analysis?"
back: "RP-HPLC uses a non-polar stationary phase (C18 or C8 alkyl chains) with a polar mobile phase. Peptides are retained based on hydrophobicity. It's preferred because it provides high resolution, reproducibility, and is compatible with MS detection."
tags: ["RP-HPLC", "chromatography", "hydrophobicity"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-ion-exchange-chromatography
front: "How does ion-exchange chromatography separate peptides?"
back: "Separates based on net charge. Cation-exchange columns retain positively charged peptides; anion-exchange retains negatively charged ones. Peptides are eluted by increasing salt concentration or changing pH to neutralize their charge."
tags: ["ion-exchange", "chromatography", "charge"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

---

id: fc-size-exclusion-chromatography
front: "What is size-exclusion chromatography (SEC)?"
back: "SEC (gel filtration) separates molecules by size. Larger peptides cannot enter the porous beads and elute first; smaller peptides enter the pores and elute later. Used for molecular weight determination and aggregate analysis."
tags: ["SEC", "chromatography", "molecular-weight"]
difficulty: "beginner"

---

---

id: fc-affinity-chromatography
front: "What is affinity chromatography?"
back: "A separation technique using a stationary phase with immobilized ligands that specifically bind the target peptide. After washing away unbound molecules, the peptide is eluted by changing conditions (pH, salt, competitive ligand)."
tags: ["affinity", "chromatography", "specificity"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-ms-basics
front: "What are the three main components of a mass spectrometer?"
back: "1) Ion source - converts molecules to gas-phase ions. 2) Mass analyzer - separates ions by m/z ratio. 3) Detector - records ion abundance. All operate under high vacuum."
tags: ["mass-spectrometry", "MS", "instrumentation"]
difficulty: "beginner"

---

id: fc-esi-ms
front: "What is ESI-MS and how does it work?"
back: "Electrospray Ionization-MS: A peptide solution is sprayed through a charged needle, producing charged droplets. As solvent evaporates, multiply charged ions are released into the gas phase. It's a soft ionization that preserves molecular integrity."
tags: ["ESI-MS", "mass-spectrometry", "ionization"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

---

id: fc-maldi-tof
front: "What is MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry?"
back: "Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption/Ionization - Time of Flight. Peptides are co-crystallized with a UV-absorbing matrix, then ionized by a laser pulse. Ions are separated by TOF - lighter ions travel faster through a field-free drift tube to the detector."
tags: ["MALDI-TOF", "mass-spectrometry", "ionization"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

---

id: fc-tandem-ms
front: "What is tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS)?"
back: "Two stages of mass analysis: first MS selects a precursor ion, which is fragmented (by collision with gas - CID), then second MS analyzes the fragment ions. Provides structural information and peptide sequence data."
tags: ["MS-MS", "tandem-MS", "fragmentation"]
difficulty: "advanced"

---

---

id: fc-peptide-sequencing-ms
front: "How are peptides sequenced by MS/MS?"
back: "Peptide ions are fragmented along the backbone, producing b-ions (N-terminal fragments) and y-ions (C-terminal fragments). The mass differences between consecutive fragments correspond to individual amino acid residues, revealing the sequence."
tags: ["sequencing", "MS-MS", "b-ions", "y-ions"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-2d-nmr
front: "What are COSY and NOESY in 2D NMR?"
back: "COSY (Correlation Spectroscopy) reveals J-coupled protons (typically 3 bonds apart) through chemical shift correlations. NOESY (Nuclear Overhauser Effect Spectroscopy) reveals protons within ~5Å through space, crucial for 3D structure determination."
tags: ["2D-NMR", "COSY", "NOESY", "structure"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-circular-dichroism
front: "What does circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy measure?"
back: "CD measures differential absorption of left and right circularly polarized light by chiral molecules. For peptides, it reveals secondary structure content: alpha-helix (222, 208 nm minima), beta-sheet (218 nm minimum), random coil (200 nm minimum)."
tags: ["CD", "secondary-structure", "spectroscopy"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-uv-vis-spectroscopy
front: "How is UV-Vis spectroscopy used for peptide analysis?"
back: "Peptides absorb UV light due to aromatic amino acids (Trp at 280nm, Tyr at 275nm, Phe at 257nm) and the peptide bond (200-220nm). Used for concentration determination, purity assessment, and folding studies."
tags: ["UV-Vis", "spectroscopy", "aromatic-amino-acids"]
difficulty: "beginner"

tags: ["fluorescence", "spectroscopy", "tryptophan"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-infrared-spectroscopy
front: "What does infrared (IR) spectroscopy reveal about peptides?"
back: "IR measures vibrational modes. The amide I band (1600-1700 cm⁻¹, mainly C=O stretch) and amide II band (1500-1600 cm⁻¹, C-N stretch + N-H bend) report on secondary structure. Useful for solid-state and membrane-bound peptides."
tags: ["IR", "infrared", "amide-bands", "secondary-structure"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-amino-acid-analysis
front: "What is amino acid analysis (AAA)?"
back: "A peptide is hydrolyzed (6M HCl, 110°C, 24h) into individual amino acids, which are separated and quantified (by HPLC or ion-exchange). Provides total composition but not sequence. Used for peptide quantification and quality control."
tags: ["AAA", "composition", "quantification"]

id: fc-edman-degradation
front: "What is Edman degradation?"
back: "A method for N-terminal sequencing of peptides. Phenylisothiocyanate (PITC) reacts with the N-terminal amino acid, which is cleaved under acidic conditions and identified by HPLC. Sequential cycles reveal the peptide sequence from the N-terminus."
tags: ["Edman", "sequencing", "N-terminal"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-peptide-mapping
front: "What is peptide mapping?"
back: "A peptide is digested with a specific protease (e.g., trypsin cleaves after Lys/Arg), and the resulting fragments are separated by HPLC and identified by MS. Used for identity confirmation, post-translational modification detection, and quality control."
tags: ["peptide-mapping", "protease-digestion", "HPLC"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

back: "Peptides are separated in a narrow capillary (25-100 μm) under an electric field based on charge-to-size ratio. Techniques include CZE (free solution), CIEF (isoelectric focusing), and CGE (gel-filled). High efficiency, small sample volumes, fast analysis."
tags: ["CE", "electrophoresis", "separation"]

back: "A label-free technique measuring real-time biomolecular interactions. One partner is immobilized on a gold sensor chip; binding of the other causes refractive index changes detected as resonance angle shifts. Provides kon, koff, and KD values."
