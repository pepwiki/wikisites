front: What is the geometry of a peptide bond?
back: "The peptide bond has partial double-bond character (resonance), making it planar and rigid. The C-N bond length is ~1.33 Å (shorter than a typical C-N single bond). The bond is usually in the trans configuration (omega angle ~180°)."

id: peptide-structure-012
front: What is a Ramachandran plot?
back: "A plot of the phi (φ) vs. psi (ψ) backbone dihedral angles of amino acid residues. It shows allowed and disallowed conformations based on steric clashes. Most residues fall in distinct regions corresponding to alpha-helix and beta-sheet conformations."
tags: [ramachandran, backbone, conformation]
difficulty: intermediate

---

id: peptide-structure-013
front: What are phi (φ) and psi (ψ) angles in protein structure?
back: "Phi (φ) is the dihedral angle around the N-Cα bond. Psi (ψ) is the dihedral angle around the Cα-C bond. Omega (ω) is around the C-N peptide bond. These three angles define the backbone conformation of each residue."
tags: [dihedral-angles, backbone, conformation]
difficulty: intermediate

id: peptide-structure-014
front: Describe the alpha (α) helix secondary structure.
back: "Right-handed helix with 3.6 residues per turn and a pitch of 5.4 Å. Stabilized by i→i+4 hydrogen bonds (C=O of residue i to N-H of residue i+4). Side chains point outward. Common in transmembrane domains and DNA-binding motifs."
tags: [secondary-structure, alpha-helix, hydrogen-bond]
difficulty: intermediate

---

---

id: peptide-structure-015
front: How does the 3₁₀ helix differ from the alpha helix?
back: "The 3₁₀ helix has 3 residues per turn (vs. 3.6 in α-helix), with i→i+3 hydrogen bonds (vs. i→i+4). It is tighter and less common, often found at the ends of α-helices. It has 10 atoms in the hydrogen-bonded ring."
tags: [secondary-structure, 310-helix, helix]
difficulty: advanced

id: peptide-structure-016
front: What is a pi (π) helix?
back: "A rare helical structure with 4.4 residues per turn and i→i+5 hydrogen bonds. It has 13 atoms in the hydrogen-bonded ring. Often found in membrane proteins and may serve as a conformational switch in enzyme catalysis."
tags: [secondary-structure, pi-helix, helix]
difficulty: advanced

id: peptide-structure-017
front: What is a parallel beta (β) sheet?
back: "Beta strands run in the same N→C direction. Hydrogen bonds between strands are angled (not perpendicular). Generally less stable than antiparallel sheets due to less optimal hydrogen bond geometry. Common in enzyme active sites."
tags: [secondary-structure, beta-sheet, parallel]
difficulty: intermediate

---

---

id: peptide-structure-018
front: What is an antiparallel beta (β) sheet?
back: "Beta strands run in opposite N→C directions. Hydrogen bonds are straight and perpendicular to the strand direction, making them more stable than parallel sheets. Common in structural proteins like silk fibroin."
tags: [secondary-structure, beta-sheet, antiparallel]
difficulty: intermediate

id: peptide-structure-019
front: What is a beta (β) turn?
back: "A tight turn connecting two beta strands, typically involving 4 residues. Stabilized by a hydrogen bond between the C=O of residue i and the N-H of residue i+3. Glycine and Proline are common at these positions due to their conformational flexibility."
tags: [secondary-structure, beta-turn, loop]
difficulty: intermediate

---

id: peptide-structure-020
front: What is a gamma (γ) turn?
back: "A 3-residue reverse turn with a hydrogen bond between the C=O of residue i and the N-H of residue i+2. Less common than beta turns. Found in globular proteins as a structural element connecting other secondary structures."
tags: [secondary-structure, gamma-turn, loop]
difficulty: advanced

front: What is an omega (Ω) loop?
back: "A loop structure where the chain reverses direction, with the beginning and end points within ~4 Å of each other. Typically 6-16 residues long. Often found on protein surfaces and involved in molecular recognition."
tags: [secondary-structure, omega-loop, loop]
difficulty: advanced

id: peptide-structure-022
front: What is a disulfide bond?
back: "A covalent bond formed between two cysteine residues (S-S linkage, ~2.05 Å). Stabilizes protein tertiary/quaternary structure. Found in extracellular proteins. Formed by oxidation; broken by reducing agents like DTT or β-mercaptoethanol."
tags: [covalent-bond, disulfide, cysteine, stability]
difficulty: intermediate

Can be positive (activation) or negative (inhibition). Classic example: hemoglobin-oxygen binding with cooperative effect."
