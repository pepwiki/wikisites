front: What is the chirality of natural amino acids and why does it matter for peptide synthesis?
back: Natural amino acids are L-enantiomers (except glycine which is achiral). The L-configuration is essential for proper peptide backbone geometry, enzyme recognition, and biological activity. D-amino acids can be used strategically to enhance metabolic stability and protease resistance.

id: peptide-comp-002
front: Describe the peptide bond formation mechanism in solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS).
back: Peptide bond formation involves nucleophilic attack of the amino group on the activated carboxyl group. Common activation methods include carbodiimides (DCC, EDC), uronium/phosphonium salts (HATU, HBTU), and active esters. The reaction proceeds through an O-acylisourea intermediate with loss of water.

id: peptide-comp-003
front: What is the role of disulfide bonds in peptide structure and how are they formed?
back: Disulfide bonds (S-S) form between cysteine residues through oxidation, stabilizing tertiary/quaternary structure. In synthetic peptides, they form via air oxidation, iodine oxidation, or DMSO-mediated oxidation. Selective formation requires orthogonal protecting groups (e.g., Acm, Trt, tBu).

id: peptide-comp-004
front: How do hydrogen bonds contribute to peptide secondary structure?
back: Hydrogen bonds between backbone C=O and N-H groups stabilize alpha-helices (i to i+4 pattern) and beta-sheets (inter-strand). These non-covalent interactions provide 2-7 kcal/mol stabilization energy. Proline disrupts helices due to cyclic side chain restricting phi angle.
tags: [hydrogen-bonds, secondary-structure, alpha-helix, beta-sheet]

id: peptide-comp-005
front: Explain hydrophobic interactions in peptide folding and stability.
back: Hydrophobic interactions drive peptide folding by minimizing nonpolar surface area exposure to water. Nonpolar residues (Leu, Ile, Val, Phe) cluster in protein interiors, contributing 1-2 kcal/mol per methylene group. This entropy-driven process is the primary force in protein folding.

id: peptide-comp-007
front: Describe van der Waals forces in peptide context.
back: Van der Waals forces include London dispersion forces (induced dipole-dipole) and Keesom forces (permanent dipole-dipole). Individually weak (0.4-4 kJ/mol), they contribute significantly cumulatively across large interfaces. Optimal packing distances are 3.4-4.0 Å for backbone atoms.

front: How do antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) exert their effects?
back: AMPs kill microbes through: (1) membrane disruption via electrostatic attraction to anionic microbial membranes, pore formation (barrel-stave, carpet, toroidal pore models); (2) intracellular targeting (DNA/protein synthesis inhibition). Examples: defensins, cathelicidins, magainins. Resistance development is slow due to membrane targeting.

id: peptide-tech-021
front: Describe the Fmoc/tBu solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) strategy.
back: Fmoc/tBu SPPS uses: (1) Fmoc-protected amino acids with acid-labile side chain protecting groups; (2) Wang, Rink, or Sieber amide resin; (3) Fmoc removal with 20% piperidine; (4) coupling with HATU/HBTU/DIC; (5) final TFA cleavage with scavengers. Cycle: deprotect→couple→wash. Typical coupling: 2-10 equivalents amino acid.
tags: [SPPS, Fmoc, synthesis, strategy]

front: What are the principles of HPLC purification for peptides?
back: Reversed-phase HPLC (RP-HPLC) separates peptides by hydrophobicity using C18/C8 columns with acetonitrile/water gradients containing 0.1% TFA. Key parameters: gradient slope, flow rate, column temperature, detection at 214-220nm. Preparative HPLC scales from mg to gram quantities. Ion-exchange and size-exclusion are alternatives.
tags: [HPLC, purification, reversed-phase, chromatography]

front: How is mass spectrometry used in peptide characterization?
back: MS techniques: (1) MALDI-TOF for molecular mass determination; (2) ESI-MS for intact mass and sequence; (3) MS/MS (CID, HCD, ETD) for sequencing; (4) LC-MS/MS for complex mixtures. Common ions: b-ions (N-terminal), y-ions (C-terminal). Accurate mass confirms sequence, identifies modifications (oxidation, deamidation).
