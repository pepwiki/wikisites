front: "What are the primary toxicity mechanisms of peptides?"
back: "Peptide toxicity mechanisms include: (1) membrane disruption via amphipathic peptides, (2) receptor-mediated signaling disruption, (3) enzyme inhibition, (4) protein aggregation, (5) mitochondrial dysfunction, (6) oxidative stress induction, and (7) immune-mediated damage. Unlike small molecules, peptides typically degrade to amino acids, reducing chronic accumulation risk."

Examples include cross-reactivity with related receptor subtypes, binding to plasma proteins altering distribution, and unintended enzyme inhibition. Sequence homology screening and receptor profiling help predict off-target risks."

Concerns include: neutralizing antibodies reducing efficacy, cross-reactivity with endogenous proteins, immune complex formation, and anaphylaxis risk. Factors influencing immunogenicity include sequence, aggregation, formulation, impurities, and route of administration."

id: fc-tox-anaphylaxis
front: "What is anaphylaxis in the context of peptide drug administration?"
back: "Anaphylaxis is a severe, rapid-onset systemic allergic reaction (Type I hypersensitivity) mediated by IgE antibodies against the peptide. Symptoms include urticaria, bronchospasm, hypotension, and potentially death. Risk factors include prior sensitization, repeated dosing, and parenteral administration. Epinephrine is the first-line treatment."

id: fc-tox-ld50
front: "What is LD50 testing and its role in peptide toxicology?"
back: "LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%) is the dose that kills 50% of test animals. Used to estimate acute toxicity potency. For peptides, LD50 is determined in rodents via the intended clinical route. Modern alternatives include fixed-dose procedures, up-and-down methods, and in vitro cytotoxicity data to reduce animal use. LD50 values inform starting doses for repeat-dose studies."

id: fc-tox-genotox
front: "What is genotoxicity and how is it assessed for peptides?"
back: "Genotoxicity is the ability to damage DNA, causing mutations or chromosomal aberrations. The standard battery includes: (1) Ames test (bacterial reverse mutation), (2) in vitro mammalian cell assay (chromosome aberration or mouse lymphoma), and (3) in vivo micronucleus test. Peptides rarely show genotoxicity due to their degradation to amino acids, but the standard battery is still required by regulators."

back: "Carcinogenicity studies (2-year rodent bioassays) are typically required when: (1) the peptide is intended for chronic use (>6 months), (2) there is structural similarity to known growth factors, (3) genotoxicity signals exist, or (4) there is repeated benign tumor induction in chronic studies. Many peptides receive waivers based on lack of concern or short treatment duration."

Biomarkers include ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin, and GGT. Hy's Law (ALT >3x ULN + bilirubin >2x ULN) predicts serious hepatotoxicity risk."

id: fc-tox-overdose
front: "How is peptide drug overdose managed?"
back: "Peptide overdose management includes: (1) immediate discontinuation, (2) supportive care (airway, breathing, circulation), (3) specific antidote administration if available, (4) enhanced elimination (hemodialysis for renally cleared peptides), and (5) monitoring for dose-dependent toxicity (hypoglycemia for insulin analogs, hypotension for vasodilatory peptides). Short half-lives of most peptides limit duration of toxicity."

id: fc-tox-therapeutic-index
front: "What is the therapeutic index and how does it apply to peptides?"
back: "The therapeutic index (TI) is the ratio of toxic dose to therapeutic dose (TD50/ED50). A higher TI indicates a wider safety margin. Peptides often have favorable TI due to: (1) specific receptor targeting, (2) rapid metabolism limiting accumulation, and (3) predictable pharmacokinetics. Narrow TI peptides (e.g., insulin, vasopressin) require careful dose titration and monitoring."

id: fc-tox-noael
front: "What is NOAEL and how is it determined for peptides?"
back: "NOAEL (No Observed Adverse Effect Level) is the highest dose at which no adverse effects are observed in toxicity studies. Determined from repeat-dose studies by identifying the dose level below the one causing statistically or biologically significant adverse effects. NOAEL is used to calculate human equivalent doses (HED) and first-in-human starting doses using allometric scaling and safety factors."

id: fc-tox-loael
front: "What is LOAEL and how does it differ from NOAEL?"
back: "LOAEL (Lowest Observed Adverse Effect Level) is the lowest dose at which adverse effects are detected. Unlike NOAEL, LOAEL always exists in a well-designed study. Types include: (1) LOAEL for minimal effects and (2) LOAEL for severe effects. When NOAEL cannot be established, LOAEL is used with additional uncertainty factors (typically 10x) for risk assessment and dose extrapolation."

back: "Idiosyncratic toxicity is unpredictable, dose-independent toxicity occurring in susceptible individuals. Mechanisms include: (1) immune-mediated (hapten hypothesis - peptide binding to self-proteins), (2) metabolic idiosyncrasy (genetic polymorphisms in metabolizing enzymes), and (3) inflammation stress hypothesis. Peptides can cause idiosyncratic reactions, particularly hepatotoxicity and skin reactions, though less common than with small molecules."

Common DLTs for peptides include: anaphylaxis, severe injection site reactions, hypotension, hypoglycemia (insulin analogs), and organ-specific toxicity. DLT definitions are protocol-specific and guide dose selection for pivotal trials. Phase I dose-escalation studies systematically identify DLTs."
