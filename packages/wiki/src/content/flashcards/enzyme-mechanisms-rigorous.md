id: fc-serine-protease-catalytic-triad
front: "Describe the catalytic triad of serine proteases and the role of each residue."
back: "The catalytic triad consists of Ser195, His57, and Asp102 (chymotrypsinogen numbering). Ser195 acts as the nucleophile, attacking the carbonyl carbon of the scissile peptide bond. His57 acts as a general base, abstracting a proton from Ser195's hydroxyl to enhance its nucleophilicity. Asp102 stabilizes the positive charge on His57 via a hydrogen bond (charge relay system). The mechanism proceeds through a tetrahedral intermediate stabilized by the oxyanion hole (backbone NH groups of Gly193 and Ser195)."

id: fc-trypsin-specificity
front: "How does trypsin achieve its substrate specificity for cleaving after lysine and arginine residues?"
back: "Trypsin cleaves peptide bonds on the C-terminal side of positively charged residues (Lys, Arg). Specificity is determined by the S1 binding pocket, which contains Asp189 at the bottom. The negatively charged carboxylate of Asp189 forms an ion pair with the positively charged side chain of Lys or Arg substrates. The S1 pocket is deep enough to accommodate the long side chains of these residues. Trypsin is activated from trypsinogen by enteropeptidase, which cleaves a hexapeptide (Val-Asp-Asp-Asp-Asp-Lys) from the N-terminus."

id: fc-chymotrypsin-specificity
front: "What structural features determine chymotrypsin's preference for aromatic and large hydrophobic residues?"
back: "Chymotrypsin preferentially cleaves after Phe, Trp, and Tyr. Its S1 pocket is large, deep, and hydrophobic, lined with nonpolar residues (Ser189, Thr226, Gly228 in the bovine enzyme). The hydrophobic pocket accommodates aromatic rings and large hydrophobic side chains via van der Waals interactions and hydrophobic stacking. Unlike trypsin, chymotrypsin lacks a negatively charged residue at position 189; instead, the pocket is lined with small, nonpolar residues. The oxyanion hole stabilizes the tetrahedral intermediate during catalysis."

id: fc-elastase-specificity
front: "How does elastase achieve specificity for small, nonpolar residues like Ala, Gly, and Ser?"
back: "Elastase cleaves after small, nonpolar residues (Ala, Gly, Val, Ser). Its S1 pocket is shallow and partially occluded by Val216 and Thr226, which replace smaller residues found in trypsin and chymotrypsin. This occlusion prevents large side chains from binding but accommodates small ones. The reduced pocket volume also explains why elastase has broader specificity than trypsin. Elastase degrades elastin, a connective tissue protein rich in Gly-Ala repeats. Mutations in the elastase gene (ELANE) cause cyclic and severe congenital neutropenia."

id: fc-thrombin-specificity
front: "What makes thrombin's substrate specificity unique among serine proteases, and what are its exosites?"
back: "Thrombin cleaves after Arg residues (Arg-bond specificity) but has additional specificity determinants: exosite I and exosite II. Exosite I (anion-binding exosite I) binds fibrinogen's Aα and Bβ chains and the thrombin receptor PAR-1, orienting substrates for cleavage. Exosite II binds heparin, which accelerates thrombin inhibition by antithrombin III. The S1 pocket contains Asp189 (like trypsin), but the 60-insertion loop (autolysis loop) and Na+ binding site further refine specificity. Thrombin is activated from prothrombin by the prothrombinase complex (Factor Xa, Va, Ca2+, phospholipids)."

id: fc-plasmin-mechanism
front: "Describe the mechanism of plasmin in fibrinolysis and how plasminogen is activated."
back: "Plasmin is a serine protease that degrades fibrin clots. It is synthesized as the zymogen plasminogen, which contains a Kringle domain that binds lysine residues on fibrin. Plasminogen is activated by tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) or urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA), which cleave an Arg561-Val562 bond. Plasmin cleaves fibrin at multiple Lys and Arg sites, producing fibrin degradation products (FDPs) including D-dimers. The catalytic triad is Ser735-His603-Asp646. α2-Antiplasmin rapidly inhibits free plasmin in the circulation."

id: fc-cysteine-protease-catalytic-dyad
front: "Describe the catalytic mechanism of cysteine proteases, including the roles of the catalytic dyad."
back: "Cysteine proteases use a catalytic dyad of Cys25 and His159 (papain numbering). Cys25 acts as the nucleophile, with its thiolate (-S−) attacking the carbonyl carbon of the substrate. His159 acts as a general acid-base, protonating the leaving group nitrogen and facilitating thiolate formation. The tetrahedral intermediate is stabilized by an oxyanion hole (Gln19 amide). The thioester acyl-enzyme intermediate is then hydrolyzed by water. The pKa of the active-site Cys is ~3.5 (lowered from ~8.3) due to the ion pair with His159."

id: fc-caspases-mechanism
front: "What are caspases, and how do their structure and mechanism relate to apoptosis?"
back: "Caspases (cysteine-aspartic proteases) cleave after Asp residues, a unique specificity among proteases. They exist as inactive zymogens (procaspases) that are activated by dimerization or cleavage. Initiator caspases (caspase-2, -8, -9, -10) activate effector caspases (caspase-3, -6, -7). The catalytic dyad is Cys-His (Cys285-His237 in caspase-3). Substrate recognition requires a tetrapeptide motif (DEVD for caspase-3). Caspases cleave >1000 substrates during apoptosis, including PARP, lamins, and ICAD (inhibitor of caspase-activated DNase)."

id: fc-cathepsins-b-l-d
front: "What are the key differences between cathepsins B, L, and D in terms of catalytic class and function?"
back: "Cathepsins are lysosomal proteases. Cathepsin B (cysteine protease, Clan CA, family C1) has an occluding loop that allows both endopeptidase and exopeptidase (carboxydipeptidase) activity. Cathepsin L (cysteine protease) is a potent endopeptidase with a wide S2 pocket preferring hydrophobic residues. Cathepsin D (aspartic protease, Clan AA, family A1) uses two Asp residues (Asp33 and Asp231) for catalysis and has an optimal pH of ~3.5. All are synthesized as preproenzymes, targeted to lysosomes via mannose-6-phosphate receptors, and activated by acidic pH."

id: fc-aspartic-protease-catalytic-dyad
front: "Describe the catalytic mechanism of aspartic proteases and the roles of the two aspartate residues."
back: "Aspartic proteases use two Asp residues (Asp32 and Asp215 in pepsin) in a catalytic dyad. One Asp acts as a general acid (protonating the scissile bond's carbonyl oxygen), while the other acts as a general base (activating a water molecule for nucleophilic attack). The two Asp residues form a symmetric active site at the interface of two β-sheet domains. The reaction proceeds through a gem-diol tetrahedral intermediate. The substrate binds in an extended β-strand conformation. Aspartic proteases are inhibited by pepstatin (a statin-containing hexapeptide) and HIV protease inhibitors."

id: fc-pepsin-mechanism
front: "What are the unique features of pepsin's catalytic mechanism and physiological role?"
back: "Pepsin is an aspartic protease active at pH 1.5–2.5 (optimal pH ~2) in the gastric lumen. It is secreted as pepsinogen (inactive zymogen) by chief cells; autoactivation occurs at low pH by cleaving a 44-residue prosegment. Pepsin cleaves preferentially at hydrophobic residues (Phe, Tyr, Leu) in the P1 and P1' positions. The active site cleft accommodates 7–8 substrate residues. Pepsin denatures above pH 6 irreversibly. It is a member of the A1 family of aspartic proteases and shares the bilobal structure with two Asp dyads."

id: fc-hiv-protease-mechanism
front: "What makes HIV protease structurally unique, and how does its C2 symmetry relate to its mechanism?"
back: "HIV protease is a C2 symmetric homodimer, with each monomer contributing one Asp residue (Asp25 and Asp25') to the catalytic dyad. This pseudo-symmetric active site resembles cellular aspartic proteases but uses two identical subunits instead of a single chain. The flap region (residues 43–58) controls substrate access and closes upon inhibitor binding. HIV protease cleaves the Gag and Gag-Pol polyproteins at 10 specific sites. Inhibitors (saquinavir, ritonavir, darunavir) mimic the tetrahedral transition state. Resistance mutations occur in the active site (I50V, V82A) and flap region."

id: fc-metalloprotease-zinc-coordination
front: "Describe the Zn2+ coordination geometry in metalloproteases and its catalytic role."
back: "In metalloproteases, Zn2+ is coordinated by three protein ligands (typically two His and one Glu) and one water molecule, forming a distorted tetrahedral geometry. The Zn2+ activates the water molecule for nucleophilic attack on the substrate's carbonyl carbon. The metal ion also polarizes the carbonyl bond, making the carbon more electrophilic. The coordination sphere is completed by a conserved methionine turn (Met-turn) that positions the catalytic residues. Chelating inhibitors (EDTA, 1,10-phenanthroline) remove Zn2+ and abolish activity."

id: fc-mmps-mechanism
front: "What are the structural and functional features of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)?"
back: "MMPs are zinc-dependent endopeptidases that degrade extracellular matrix (ECM) components. All MMPs share: (1) a signal peptide, (2) a prodomain with a Cys-switch (cysteine residue that coordinates Zn2+ in the zymogen), (3) a catalytic domain with Zn2+ coordinated by three His (HEXXHXXGXXH motif), and (4) a hemopexin domain. The Cys-switch mechanism: in the zymogen, the prodomain Cys blocks the active site; activation disrupts this interaction. MMPs are inhibited by TIMPs (tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases). MMP-2 and MMP-9 (gelatinases) contain fibronectin type II repeats for collagen binding."

id: fc-proteasome-mechanism
front: "Describe the catalytic mechanism of the 20S proteasome core particle."
back: "The 20S proteasome core particle is a barrel-shaped complex of four stacked rings (α7β7β7α7). Each β-subunit contains the catalytic site with an N-terminal threonine (Thr1) as the nucleophile. Three catalytic activities are present: β1 (caspase-like, cleaves after Asp), β2 (trypsin-like, cleaves after Lys/Arg), and β5 (chymotrypsin-like, cleaves after hydrophobic residues). The mechanism: Thr1-Oγ attacks the carbonyl carbon; Thr1-NH2 acts as the proton shuttle. The 19S regulatory particle recognizes ubiquitinated substrates and unfolds them. Proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib, carfilzomib) covalently modify Thr1."

id: fc-kinase-mechanism
front: "What are the structural and mechanistic differences between Ser/Thr kinases and Tyr kinases?"
back: "All kinases transfer a γ-phosphate from ATP to substrate hydroxyl groups. Ser/Thr kinases (e.g., PKA, CaMKII) have a catalytic loop with Asp166 (PKA numbering) as the general base that deprotonates the substrate Ser/Thr. Tyr kinases (e.g., EGFR, Src) use an analogous Asp but position it differently due to the larger Tyr substrate. Key conserved motifs: glycine-rich loop (P-loop, phosphate binding), catalytic loop (HRD motif), activation loop (DFG motif, phosphorylation-dependent). Ser/Thr kinases phosphorylate Ser/Thr in flexible loops; Tyr kinases have a deeper active site cleft to accommodate the aromatic ring."

id: fc-phosphatase-mechanism
front: "What are the two main catalytic mechanisms used by protein phosphatases?"
back: "Two major classes: (1) Cys-based phosphatases (PTPs, PTP1B) use a conserved Cys (Cys215 in PTP1B) as the nucleophile, forming a thiophosphate intermediate; the conserved Asp (Asp181) acts as a general acid. The signature motif is HC(X)5R (P-loop). (2) Asp-based phosphatases (PP1, PP2A, PP2C/Mn-dependent) use a binuclear metal center (Fe2+/Mn2+ or Mn2+/Mn2+) with Asp/Glu ligands; a metal-activated water acts as the nucleophile. PP1 and PP2A are Ser/Thr phosphatases; PTPs are primarily Tyr phosphatases but include dual-specificity phosphatases (DUSPs)."

id: fc-glycosyltransferase-mechanism
front: "What are the retaining and inverting mechanisms of glycosyltransferases?"
back: "Glycosyltransferases (GTs) transfer sugar moieties from activated donors (e.g., UDP-glucose) to acceptors. Two mechanistic classes: (1) Inverting GTs use a single-displacement SN2-like mechanism with general base-catalyzed activation of the acceptor hydroxyl, resulting in inversion of configuration at the anomeric carbon. (2) Retaining GTs use a double-displacement mechanism: first, the donor's anomeric oxygen is displaced by a catalytic nucleophile (Asp or Glu), forming a covalent glycosyl-enzyme intermediate; second, the acceptor displaces the enzyme, retaining stereochemistry. Some retaining GTs use a front-face SNi-like mechanism."

id: fc-ubiquitin-e1-activation
front: "Describe the two-step activation mechanism of ubiquitin by E1 activating enzymes."
back: "E1 (ubiquitin-activating enzyme, UBA1/UBA6) activates ubiquitin in two steps: (1) Adenylation: E1 catalyzes ubiquitin C-terminal adenylation (Ub-AMP) using ATP, with release of PPi. (2) Thioester formation: A catalytic Cys in E1 attacks Ub-AMP, forming a thioester bond (E1~Ub) with release of AMP. The E1 then transfers Ub to E2 via a transthioesterification reaction. Human cells have 2 E1 enzymes (UBA1, UBA6) but ~40 E2s and >600 E3s. The adenylation and thioester domains of E1 undergo large conformational changes during the catalytic cycle."

id: fc-ubiquitin-e2-conjugation
front: "What is the catalytic mechanism of E2 conjugating enzymes in ubiquitin transfer?"
back: "E2 conjugating enzymes (UBCs) accept activated ubiquitin from E1 via transthioesterification, forming an E2~Ub thioester intermediate. The E2's surface residues (around the Ile44 hydrophobic patch of Ub) determine chain linkage specificity (K48, K63, K11, etc.). UBE2D/UbcH5 family has broad specificity; UBE2N (Ubc13) forms K63-linked chains."
