back: "1) Serine proteases (serine nucleophile), 2) Cysteine proteases (cysteine nucleophile), 3) Aspartic proteases (aspartate-activated water), 4) Metalloproteases (metal-activated water). Each family uses a different catalytic strategy to hydrolyze peptide bonds."

id: fc-serine-protease-mechanism
front: "Describe the catalytic mechanism of serine proteases."
back: "Uses a catalytic triad (Ser-His-Asp). 1) Serine oxygen attacks peptide bond carbonyl, forming tetrahedral intermediate. 2) Histidine acts as general base/acid. 3) Aspartate stabilizes histidine. 4) Acyl-enzyme intermediate forms, then water hydrolyzes it. Examples: trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase."
tags: ["metabolism", "proteases", "serine-proteases", "catalytic-mechanism"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-cysteine-protease-mechanism
front: "How do cysteine proteases catalyze peptide bond hydrolysis?"
back: "Uses cysteine thiol (-SH) as nucleophile instead of serine. Histidine acts as general base to activate cysteine. Forms thioester intermediate (vs oxygen ester in serine proteases). Requires reducing environment for active thiol. Examples: caspases, cathepsins, calpains."
tags: ["metabolism", "proteases", "cysteine-proteases", "catalytic-mechanism"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-aspartic-protease-mechanism
front: "What is the mechanism of aspartic proteases?"
back: "Two aspartate residues activate a water molecule for direct nucleophilic attack on the peptide bond. No covalent intermediate forms - proceeds through a tetrahedral intermediate that collapses. Operates optimally at acidic pH. Examples: pepsin, HIV protease, renin, beta-secretase (BACE)."
tags: ["metabolism", "proteases", "aspartic-proteases", "catalytic-mechanism"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-metalloprotease-mechanism
front: "How do metalloproteases cleave peptide bonds?"
back: "Use a divalent metal ion (usually Zn2+) coordinated by histidine residues. Metal activates a water molecule for nucleophilic attack. Glutamate residue acts as general acid-base. No covalent intermediate. Examples: matrix metalloproteases (MMPs), carboxypeptidase A, thermolysin, angiotensin-converting enzyme."

id: fc-proteasome-20s
front: "Describe the structure and function of the 20S proteasome core."
back: "Barrel-shaped structure with 4 stacked rings: 2 outer alpha-rings (gate access) and 2 inner beta-rings (catalytic sites). Beta-rings have 3 catalytic activities: chymotrypsin-like (hydrophobic), trypsin-like (basic), and caspase-like (acidic). Uses threonine protease mechanism. Degrades proteins into 3-25 residue peptides."
tags: ["metabolism", "proteasome", "intracellular-degradation"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-proteasome-19s
front: "What is the role of the 19S regulatory particle in the 26S proteasome?"
back: "Caps one or both ends of the 20S core. Functions: 1) Recognizes ubiquitinated substrates via ubiquitin receptors. 2) Removes ubiquitin chains (deubiquitinases). 3) Unfolds substrate using ATPase activity (6 ATPase ring). 4) Opens the 20S gate and translocates substrate into core. Requires ATP for function."

id: fc-ubiquitin-tagging
front: "How does ubiquitin tagging target proteins for degradation?"
back: "Ubiquitin (76 aa protein) is attached to lysine residues on target proteins via isopeptide bond. Polyubiquitin chain (K48-linked, min 4 ubiquitins) is the canonical degradation signal. Process: activation (E1) -> conjugation (E2) -> ligation (E3). 26S proteasome recognizes and degrades tagged proteins."

id: fc-e1-e2-e3-enzymes
front: "What are the roles of E1, E2, and E3 enzymes in ubiquitination?"
back: "E1 (ubiquitin-activating): activates ubiquitin with ATP, forms thioester bond (~2 in humans). E2 (ubiquitin-conjugating): accepts ubiquitin from E1 (~40 in humans). E3 (ubiquitin ligase): recognizes substrate and transfers ubiquitin from E2 (~600+ in humans). E3 provides substrate specificity. Two types: HECT (intermediate) and RING (direct transfer)."

id: fc-lysosomal-degradation
front: "How do lysosomes degrade peptides and proteins?"
back: "Lysosomes maintain acidic pH (4.5-5.0) via V-ATPase proton pump. Contain ~60 hydrolytic enzymes (cathepsins B, D, L, H, etc.) covering all protease classes. Substrates delivered via endocytosis, phagocytosis, or autophagy. Cathepsin D (aspartic) and cathepsin B/L (cysteine) are primary endopeptidases. Products exported to cytosol via transporters."

id: fc-intestinal-absorption
front: "How are peptides absorbed in the intestine?"
back: "Most dietary protein is digested to amino acids and small peptides (2-3 aa) before absorption. Oligopeptides absorbed via PepT1 (H+-coupled symporter) in jejunum/ileum. id: fc-brush-border-enzymes
front: "What are the major brush border peptidases?"
back: "Located on enterocyte apical membrane: 1) Aminopeptidase N (CD13) - removes N-terminal amino acids. 2) Aminopeptidase A - acidic N-terminal residues. 3) Dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-4) - cleaves X-Pro/Ala dipeptides. 4) Endopeptidase 24.11 (neprilysin). 5) Gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase. These complete digestion of luminal peptides."

id: fc-dpp4
front: "What is dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-4) and why is it clinically important?"
back: "Serine protease that cleaves X-Pro or X-Ala dipeptides from N-terminus. Expressed on brush border, endothelial cells, lymphocytes. Key substrate: incretin hormones GLP-1 and GIP (half-life ~2 min). DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, saxagliptin) extend incretin half-life for diabetes treatment. Also called CD26."

id: fc-ace
front: "What is angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) and its substrates?"
back: "Zinc metalloprotease on pulmonary/renal endothelium. Functions: 1) Converts angiotensin I -> II (removes C-terminal His-Leu). 2) Degrades bradykinin. 3) Cleaves substance P, enkephalins. ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril) block both functions - lower BP and cause dry cough (bradykinin accumulation)."

id: fc-endopeptidases
front: "What are endopeptidases and how do they differ from exopeptidases?"
back: "Endopeptidases cleave internal peptide bonds within the polypeptide chain. Examples: pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, matrix metalloproteases. Exopeptidases remove amino acids from chain termini. Endopeptidases initiate protein breakdown; exopeptidases complete degradation to free amino acids."
tags: ["metabolism", "endopeptidases", "protease-classification"]
difficulty: "beginner"

id: fc-exopeptidases
front: "What are exopeptidases and their two main types?"
back: "Exopeptidases remove amino acids from polypeptide termini. Two types: 1) Aminopeptidases - cleave from N-terminus. 2) Carboxypeptidases - cleave from C-terminus. Work processively one residue at a time. Produce free amino acids and dipeptides. Essential for completing digestion after endopeptidase action."

id: fc-renal-clearance
front: "How do kidneys clear peptides from circulation?"
back: "Kidneys clear peptides via: 1) Glomerular filtration (molecules <60 kDa freely filtered). 2) Proximal tubular reabsorption by endocytosis (megalin/cubilin receptors). 3) Intraluminal degradation by brush border peptidases. 4) Peritubular extraction. Small peptides (<500 Da) filtered and reabsorbed; larger peptides filtered less efficiently."

id: fc-glomerular-filtration
front: "What determines glomerular filtration of peptides?"
back: "Filtration depends on: 1) Molecular size (cutoff ~60 kDa, effective <5-8 kDa). 2) Charge (cationic filtered more easily, anionic restricted). 3) Shape. 4) Protein binding. GFR ~125 mL/min. Peptides >10 kDa poorly filtered. Most therapeutic peptides (1-10 kDa) are freely filtered, contributing to short half-life."

id: fc-tubular-reabsorption
front: "How are filtered peptides reabsorbed in renal tubules?"
back: "Proximal tubule cells reabsorb peptides via: 1) Megalin/cubilin receptor-mediated endocytosis for small peptides/proteins. 2) Peptide transporters (PepT1, PepT2) for di/tripeptides. 3) Intracellular lysosomal degradation after reabsorption. PepT2 is high-affinity, low-capacity; dominant in S3 segment. Reabsorbed peptides are degraded, amino acids returned to blood."

id: fc-hepatic-metabolism
front: "How does the liver metabolize peptides?"
back: "Hepatic metabolism involves: 1) Kupffer cell endocytosis/phagocytosis for larger peptides. 2) Hepatocyte uptake via specific receptors and transporters. 3) Lysosomal degradation (cathepsins). 4) Cytoplasmic peptidases. 5) Biliary excretion of peptide fragments. First-pass metabolism is significant for orally administered peptides. Liver is primary site for larger peptide (>5 kDa) clearance."

id: fc-bbb
front: "How do peptides cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB)?"
back: "BBB limits peptide entry due to tight junctions and efflux transporters. Peptides cross via: 1) Receptor-mediated transcytosis (insulin, transferrin receptors). 2) Adsorptive transcytosis (cationic peptides bind negatively charged membrane). 3) Circumventricular organs (area postrema, median eminence lack BBB). 4) Intranasal route. Most peptides have very low CNS penetration."

id: fc-deamidation
front: "What is deamidation and which amino acids are affected?"
back: "Deamidation converts asparagine (Asn) to aspartate/isoaspartate, and glutamine (Gln) to glutamate. Asn is much more susceptible than Gln. Mechanism: nucleophilic attack by adjacent backbone nitrogen on Asn side chain, forming succinimide intermediate. Rate depends on: sequence context (Asn-Gly fastest), pH (optimal ~7-8), temperature, ionic strength. Introduces negative charge, alters structure."
tags: ["metabolism", "deamidation", "asparagine", "chemical-degradation"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-oxidation
front: "Which amino acids are susceptible to oxidation in peptides?"
back: "Primary targets: 1) Methionine -> methionine sulfoxide (most susceptible). 2) Cysteine -> cysteine sulfinic/sulfonic acid, disulfide scrambling. 3) Tryptophan -> oxindolalanine, kynurenine. 4) Histidine -> 2-oxo-histidine. 5) Tyrosine -> DOPA. Oxidation triggered by: H2O2, metal ions (Fe, Cu), light, peroxides. Affects bioactivity and immunogenicity."

id: fc-racemization
front: "What is racemization in peptides and why does it matter?"
back: "Racemization converts L-amino acids to D-amino acids, losing biological activity since most proteases only cleave L-amino acid bonds. Susceptible residues: Asp > Ser > Ala > others. Mechanism: alpha-proton abstraction forming planar carbanion. Rate increases with: high pH, elevated temperature, metal ions. D-amino acid-containing peptides resist proteolysis but may be immunogenic."
tags: ["metabolism", "racemization", "D-amino-acids", "chemical-degradation"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-amyloid-formation
front: "What is amyloid formation and how does it relate to peptide metabolism?"
back: "Amyloid fibrils are cross-beta-sheet aggregates resistant to proteolytic degradation. Form when peptides misfold and aggregate (nucleation-elongation mechanism). Examples: Abeta (Alzheimer's), alpha-synuclein (Parkinson's), islet amyloid polypeptide (type 2 diabetes), transthyretin (cardiac amyloidosis). Proteases like neprilysin degrade Abeta; failure of clearance contributes to disease."
