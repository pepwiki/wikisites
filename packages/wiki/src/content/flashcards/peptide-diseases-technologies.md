front: "What is the molecular basis of prion diseases?"
back: "Prion diseases involve conformational conversion of normal cellular prion protein (PrPC, alpha-helix rich) into pathogenic PrPSc (beta-sheet rich). PrPSc acts as a template to recruit and convert more PrPC, leading to exponential propagation of misfolded protein. This causes spongiform neurodegeneration in diseases such as Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD), bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE), and scrapie."

id: fc-peptide-disease-huntington
front: "How does polyglutamine expansion cause Huntington's disease?"
back: "Huntington's disease results from CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion (>36 repeats) in the HTT gene, producing an abnormally long polyglutamine (polyQ) tract in the huntingtin protein. The expanded polyQ tract causes huntingtin to misfold and form intracellular aggregates (inclusion bodies) in neurons, particularly in the striatum. This leads to transcriptional dysregulation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and progressive neurodegeneration causing chorea and cognitive decline."

id: fc-peptide-disease-alzheimer-amyloid
front: "What is amyloid-beta and how does it contribute to Alzheimer's disease?"
back: "Amyloid-beta (Abeta) is a 38-43 amino acid peptide generated by sequential cleavage of amyloid precursor protein (APP) by beta-secretase (BACE1) and gamma-secretase. Abeta42 is the most aggregation-prone species. It forms soluble oligomers and insoluble fibrils that deposit as extracellular amyloid plaques. Oligomeric Abeta is neurotoxic, impairing synaptic plasticity, triggering neuroinflammation, and promoting tau hyperphosphorylation. The amyloid cascade hypothesis posits Abeta accumulation as the initiating event in Alzheimer's pathogenesis."

id: fc-peptide-disease-alzheimer-tau
front: "What role does tau protein play in Alzheimer's disease?"
back: "Tau is a microtubule-associated protein that stabilizes axonal microtubules. In Alzheimer's disease, tau becomes abnormally hyperphosphorylated by kinases including GSK-3beta and CDK5. Hyperphosphorylated tau detaches from microtubules, misfolds, and aggregates into paired helical filaments forming intracellular neurofibrillary tangles. Tau pathology spreads in a prion-like manner along neural circuits. Tau pathology correlates more strongly with cognitive decline than amyloid plaque burden, making it an important therapeutic target."

front: "What is AL amyloidosis and what peptide is involved?"
back: "AL (light chain) amyloidosis is the most common systemic amyloidosis in developed countries. Monoclonal immunoglobulin light chains (kappa or lambda) produced by clonal plasma cells misfold and deposit as amyloid fibrils in organs including heart, kidneys, liver, and peripheral nerves. The light chain variable domain contains mutations that destabilize the native fold, promoting amyloid formation. Treatment targets the underlying plasma cell clone with chemotherapy or autologous stem cell transplant."

id: fc-peptide-tech-spps
front: "What is solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS)?"
back: "SPPS, developed by Merrifield in 1963, builds peptides on an insoluble resin support. The C-terminal amino acid is anchored to the resin via a linker. Iterative cycles of Fmoc deprotection (piperidine) and amino acid coupling (activated esters) extend the chain. After completion, TFA cleavage simultaneously removes side-chain protecting groups and releases the peptide from resin. Key advantages: simplified purification (filtration washes), automation compatibility, and scalability. Common resins include Wang, Rink amide, and Sieber amide."
tags: ["technology", "SPPS", "peptide-synthesis", "Fmoc", "Merrifield"]

id: fc-peptide-tech-native-chemical-ligation
front: "What is native chemical ligation (NCL)?"
back: "NCL is a chemoselective reaction joining two unprotected peptide fragments. Fragment 1 has a C-terminal thioester; Fragment 2 has an N-terminal cysteine. The reaction proceeds in two steps: (1) reversible transthioesterification between the thioester and cysteine thiol, (2) irreversible S-to-N acyl shift forming a native peptide bond at the ligation site. This enables synthesis of proteins >100 amino acids by assembling shorter synthetic fragments. Combined with expressed protein ligation (EPL) using intein-expressed thioesters, NCL enables semi-synthesis of modified proteins."

front: "How does microwave-assisted peptide synthesis improve SPPS?"
back: "Microwave irradiation provides rapid, uniform dielectric heating of polar molecules in the reaction mixture, accelerating both coupling reactions and Fmoc deprotection steps. Benefits include: (1) reduced reaction times from hours to minutes, (2) improved crude peptide purity by minimizing aggregation and side reactions, (3) more efficient synthesis of difficult sequences (e.g., beta-amyloid fragments), and (4) reduced racemization compared to prolonged conventional heating. Commercial microwave peptide synthesizers (CEM Liberty Blue, Biotage Initiator+) are widely used."

id: fc-peptide-tech-click-chemistry
front: "How is click chemistry used in peptide modification?"
back: "Copper-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) forms 1,4-disubstituted 1,2,3-triazole rings from azides and terminal alkynes. In peptide chemistry, triazoles serve as: (1) metabolically stable bioisosteres of amide bonds, (2) macrocyclization linkers for cyclic peptides, (3) conjugation handles for attaching fluorophores, PEG chains, or targeting moieties. Strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition (SPAAC) eliminates the need for cytotoxic copper catalyst, enabling bioorthogonal labeling in living systems."

back: "Lipidation involves conjugating fatty acid chains (typically C12-C18) to peptides via chemical linkers. This modification enables reversible non-covalent binding to serum albumin after injection, creating a circulating depot that shields the peptide from proteolytic degradation and renal filtration. Examples: liraglutide (C16 fatty acid, once-daily GLP-1 RA), semaglutide (C18 fatty diacid, once-weekly GLP-1 RA), insulin detemir (C14 fatty acid). id: fc-peptide-computational-md
front: "What is molecular dynamics (MD) simulation of peptides?"
back: "MD simulation numerically integrates Newton's equations of motion (F = ma) for every atom in a peptide-solvent system. Forces are calculated from empirical force fields (AMBER, CHARMM, OPLS) modeling bonded interactions (bonds, angles, dihedrals) and non-bonded interactions (electrostatics, van der Waals). Timestep integration (1-2 fs) propagates positions and velocities over nanosecond to microsecond timescales. MD reveals conformational dynamics, folding pathways, binding mechanisms, and thermodynamic properties. Enhanced sampling methods (replica exchange, metadynamics) overcome timescale limitations."
tags: ["computational", "molecular-dynamics", "simulation", "force-field"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-peptide-computational-force-field
front: "What is a force field in computational peptide chemistry?"
back: "A force field is a set of mathematical functions and parameterized values that describe the potential energy of a molecular system as a function of atomic positions. Components include: bonded terms (bond stretching, angle bending, torsional rotation) and non-bonded terms (Lennard-Jones van der Waals, Coulombic electrostatics). Major force fields for peptides: AMBER (ff14SB, ff19SB), CHARMM (CHARMM36m), OPLS-AA/M. Parameters are derived from quantum mechanical calculations and experimental data (crystal structures, spectroscopy, thermodynamic measurements)."

front: "What is computational peptide-protein docking?"
back: "Peptide-protein docking predicts the binding pose and affinity of a peptide to a target protein. Rigid-body docking (ZDOCK, ClusPro) treats the peptide as rigid; flexible docking (FlexPepDock, HADDOCK) allows backbone and sidechain sampling. Scoring functions evaluate shape complementarity, electrostatics, hydrogen bonding, and desolvation. Challenges include peptide flexibility, induced fit, and water-mediated interactions. Applications: mapping protein-protein interactions, designing peptide inhibitors, and identifying binding hot spots for drug design."

id: fc-peptide-computational-fep
front: "What is free energy perturbation (FEP) in peptide drug design?"
back: "FEP is a rigorous statistical mechanics method that computes relative free energy differences between two chemical states by alchemically transforming one molecule into another through intermediate states. For peptide drug design, FEP predicts how sequence modifications affect binding affinity to a target protein. The Zwanzig equation relates the free energy difference to ensemble averages over the perturbation. FEP achieves chemical accuracy (~1 kcal/mol) when properly converged, enabling rational optimization of lead peptides without synthesizing every analog."

front: "How does AlphaFold2 predict protein and peptide structures?"
back: "AlphaFold2 uses a novel Transformer-based neural network architecture with Evoformer modules that process multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) and residue pair representations. It learns evolutionary coevolutionary signals from MSAs to infer spatial contacts and orientations. The structure module converts these representations into 3D coordinates through iterative refinement. AlphaFold2 achieves near-experimental accuracy (median GDT ~92 on CASP14), revolutionizing structural biology. It predicts static structures and provides confidence scores (pLDDT, PAE) but does not directly model dynamics."

id: fc-peptide-structure-alpha-helix
front: "What defines an alpha-helix in peptide structure?"
back: "The alpha-helix is a right-handed helical secondary structure with 3.6 residues per turn and a pitch of 5.4 Angstroms. Backbone dihedral angles are approximately phi = -57 degrees, psi = -47 degrees. It is stabilized by i to i+4 backbone hydrogen bonds (C=O of residue i to N-H of residue i+4). The helix has a dipole moment (positive at N-terminus, negative at C-terminus). Proline disrupts alpha-helices due to its rigid ring structure and lack of amide hydrogen. Common in transmembrane domains and leucine zippers."
tags: ["structure", "alpha-helix", "secondary-structure", "Ramachandran"]
difficulty: "beginner"

id: fc-peptide-structure-beta-sheet
front: "What defines a beta-sheet in peptide structure?"
back: "Beta-sheets consist of extended beta-strands connected laterally by backbone hydrogen bonds (C=O of one strand to N-H of adjacent strand). Two types: parallel (strands run same direction, angled H-bonds) and antiparallel (strands run opposite directions, linear H-bonds). Beta-strands have phi ~-139 degrees, psi ~+135 degrees. Beta-sheets form the core of many globular proteins and are central to amyloid fibril structure. Beta-turns and beta-hairpins connect strands. Edge strands can be aggregation-prone."

id: fc-peptide-structure-disulfide
front: "What are disulfide bonds and how do they stabilize peptide structure?"
back: "Disulfide bonds are covalent -S-S- bridges formed between the thiol groups of two cysteine residues through oxidation. They stabilize tertiary and quaternary structure by cross-linking different regions of the polypeptide chain. In vivo, disulfide bonds form in the endoplasmic reticulum catalyzed by protein disulfide isomerase (PDI). In synthetic peptides, directed disulfide formation uses orthogonal Cys protecting groups (Acm, Trt, StBu) and selective oxidation. Incorrect disulfide pairing causes misfolding. Critical in toxins, hormones (insulin), and antimicrobial peptides."
tags: ["structure", "disulfide-bond", "cysteine", "stabilization"]

id: fc-peptide-drug-design-peptidomimetic
front: "What are peptidomimetics and why are they needed?"
back: "Peptidomimetics are molecules that mimic the 3D arrangement and biological activity of peptides while overcoming their limitations: rapid proteolytic degradation, poor oral bioavailability, and limited membrane permeability. Strategies include: (1) non-natural amino acids (beta-amino acids, D-amino acids), (2) backbone modifications (reduced amide, retro-inverso), (3) scaffold replacements (benzodiazepines, beta-turn mimetics), (4) macrocyclic constraints. Examples: ACE inhibitors (captopril, enalapril), HIV protease inhibitors (saquinavir), and integrin antagonists (tirofiban)."

id: fc-peptide-drug-design-stapled
front: "What are stapled peptides and how do they improve drug properties?"
back: "Stapled peptides contain an all-hydrocarbon crosslink formed by ring-closing metathesis of two alpha-methylated, alpha-alkenyl amino acids (typically at i, i+4 or i, i+7 positions). The staple constrains the peptide into an alpha-helical conformation, improving: (1) binding affinity to targets like MDM2, BCL-2, beta-catenin, (2) protease resistance (reduced flexibility), (3) cell permeability (helix stabilization reduces polar surface area). ALRN-6924 (MDM2/MDMX inhibitor) entered clinical trials. Limitations include molecular weight and synthetic complexity."

id: fc-peptide-drug-design-cyclic
front: "What are the advantages of cyclic peptides as therapeutics?"
back: "Cyclic peptides offer several advantages: (1) enhanced metabolic stability (no free N/C-termini for exopeptidases), (2) improved binding affinity through conformational pre-organization, (3) increased membrane permeability via intramolecular hydrogen bonding (chameleonic behavior), (4) larger binding surfaces for protein-protein interactions compared to small molecules. Examples: cyclosporin A (immunosuppressant, orally bioavailable), vancomycin (antibiotic), octreotide (somatostatin analog). Cyclization strategies: head-to-tail amide, disulfide, thioether, triazole (click chemistry), lactam bridges."

id: fc-peptide-drug-design-conjugate
front: "What are peptide-drug conjugates (PDCs)?"
back: "PDCs consist of a peptide (targeting moiety) linked to a cytotoxic payload via a cleavable or non-cleavable linker. The peptide directs the conjugate to a specific cell surface receptor for receptor-mediated endocytosis. Linkers release the payload inside the cell (acid-labile, protease-cleavable, or disulfide-based). PDC advantages over ADCs: smaller size, better tumor penetration, lower immunogenicity, easier synthesis. Examples: ANG1005 (paclitaxel-conjugated to angiopep-2 for brain tumors), BT1718 (DM1 conjugated to a bicyclic peptide targeting MT1-MMP)."

front: "How do GLP-1 receptor agonists treat type 2 diabetes?"
back: "GLP-1 receptor agonists mimic the incretin hormone GLP-1 (7-36 amide, 30 amino acids) which is secreted by intestinal L-cells after meals. They activate GLP-1 receptors on pancreatic beta cells to stimulate insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner (reducing hypoglycemia risk), suppress glucagon secretion, slow gastric emptying, and promote satiety via hypothalamic action. Native GLP-1 is rapidly degraded by DPP-4 (half-life ~2 minutes). Modified analogs (exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide, tirzepatide) resist degradation and extend half-life."
