front: "What are cyclic peptides and what advantages do they offer over linear peptides?"
back: "Cyclic peptides have their N- and C-termini (or side chains) joined to form a ring. Advantages: 1) constrained conformation improves target selectivity, 2) enhanced metabolic stability against exopeptidases, 3) improved membrane permeability in some cases, 4) higher binding affinity due to pre-organized structure. Examples: cyclosporine A, vancomycin."

front: "What are peptide aptamers and how are they selected?"
back: "Peptide aptamers are short constrained peptides (10-20 aa) displayed on a scaffold protein (e.g., thioredoxin) that bind targets with high affinity and specificity. Selected through yeast two-hybrid or phage display against intracellular targets. They function as competitive inhibitors, blocking protein-protein interactions. More stable than antibodies intracellularly."

front: "What are peptidomimetics and what structural modifications are commonly used?"
back: "Peptidomimetics are molecules that mimic peptide structure/function but have modified backbones. Common modifications: 1) N-methylation (blocks proteases, improves permeability), 2) beta-amino acids (resistant to proteases), 3) urea-based peptidomimetics, 4) azapeptides, 5) reduced amide bonds. They combine drug-like properties of small molecules with peptide binding specificity."

back: "Multivalent peptides present multiple binding epitopes on a scaffold (dendrimer, polymer, nanoparticle). Avidity effect: simultaneous binding to multiple receptors increases apparent affinity 100-1000x. Used for targeting clustered receptors (integrins, receptor tyrosine kinases). Examples: RGD dendrimers for integrin targeting, multivalent antimicrobial peptides."

front: "How is NMR used in peptide drug design?"
back: "NMR determines peptide 3D structure in solution, reveals dynamics, and maps binding interfaces. Techniques: 1) NOESY for distance constraints, 2) chemical shift perturbation for binding site mapping, 3) STD-NMR for weak ligand binding, 4) trNOE for bound conformation. Advantage over X-ray: works in physiologically relevant conditions without crystallization."

id: fc-peptide-stability-deamidation
front: "What is peptide deamidation and how can it be prevented?"
back: "Deamidation is the conversion of Asn/Gln to Asp/Glu, occurring via succinimide intermediate. Rate depends on: 1) sequence (Asn-Gly fastest), 2) pH (maximum at pH 5), 3) temperature, 4) buffer. Prevention: 1) substitute Asn with Asp/Gln at non-critical positions, 2) use low temperature storage, 3) optimize pH, 4) add stabilizers (sucrose, sorbitol), 5) avoid Asn-Gly sequences in design."
tags: ["stability", "deamidation", "Asn", "formulation"]

id: fc-peptide-stability-oxidation
front: "Which amino acids are susceptible to oxidation and how is it prevented?"
back: "Oxidizable residues: Met (to sulfoxide), Cys (to disulfide/sulfenic acid), Trp (multiple products), His. Prevention strategies: 1) exclude Met/Cys if non-essential, 2) add antioxidants (methionine as sacrificial oxidant, BHT), 3) nitrogen overlay in manufacturing, 4) lyophilization, 5) avoid metal ions (chelators like EDTA), 6) low temperature, dark storage."

front: "What are the critical parameters for peptide lyophilization?"
back: "Lyophilization (freeze-drying) stabilizes peptides by removing water. Critical parameters: 1) freezing temperature (must be below Tg'), 2) annealing step to improve crystal structure, 3) primary drying (sublimation at low pressure), 4) secondary drying (desorption). Excipients: sucrose/trehalose (amorphous bulking), mannitol (crystalline). Must maintain cake structure and avoid collapse temperature excursions."

id: fc-peptide-stability-aggregation
front: "What causes peptide aggregation and what strategies prevent it?"
back: "Aggregation pathways: nucleation-dependent (lag phase then rapid growth), concentration-dependent, surface-mediated. Prevention: 1) reduce concentration, 2) add surfactants (polysorbate 20/80 - surface competition), 3) adjust pH away from pI, 4) use co-solvents (arginine, urea), 5) control temperature, 6) minimize agitation/shaking. Monitors: SEC-HPLC, DLS, CD spectroscopy for early detection."

id: fc-peptide-stability-ph
front: "How does pH affect peptide stability and formulation design?"
back: "pH impacts: 1) deamidation rate (optimal at pH 5, slower at pH <4 or >7), 2) hydrolysis of Asp-Pro bonds (acid-labile), 3) oxidation (metal-catalyzed at neutral pH), 4) aggregation near pI. Formulation pH selection: balance chemical stability vs solubility vs biological activity. Buffers: acetate (pH 4-5), phosphate (pH 6-7.5), histidine (pH 5.5-6.5). Avoid citrate with peptides."

id: fc-peptide-analysis-hplc
front: "What HPLC methods are used for peptide analysis and what do they measure?"
back: "HPLC modes for peptides: 1) RP-C18 (purity, identity, hydrophobicity), 2) SEC (aggregates, fragments), 3) IEX (charge variants), 4) HILIC (glycopeptides). RP-HPLC most common: gradient elution with ACN/water + TFA. SEC-HPLC separates by size (aggregates > monomer > fragments). IEX detects deamidation (extra negative charge). UV detection at 215 nm (backbone) or 280 nm (Trp/Tyr)."

id: fc-peptide-analysis-cd
front: "What does circular dichroism spectroscopy reveal about peptide structure?"
back: "CD measures differential absorption of left/right circularly polarized light. Far-UV CD (190-250 nm): secondary structure content. Alpha-helix: double minima at 208, 222 nm. Beta-sheet: minimum at 218 nm. Random coil: minimum at ~200 nm. Near-UV CD (250-350 nm): tertiary structure (aromatic residues). Applications: stability studies (Tm determination), binding assays, formulation screening."

back: "ELISA formats for peptides: 1) competitive (most common for small peptides <3 kDa), 2) sandwich (requires two epitopes - rare for short peptides), 3) bridging (anti-idiotype for PK studies). id: fc-peptide-clinical-pk
front: "What are typical pharmacokinetic properties of peptide drugs?"
back: "Peptide PK characteristics: 1) short half-life (minutes to hours for linear peptides), 2) renal clearance (MW <60 kDa), 3) proteolytic metabolism (plasma, liver, kidney), 4) low oral bioavailability (<2% typically), 5) Vss ~0.1-0.3 L/kg (hydrophilic). Modifications to improve PK: PEGylation (increases half-life), albumin binding, lipidation, Fc fusion. SC absorption: slow, Tmax 2-8h, bioavailability 50-90%."
