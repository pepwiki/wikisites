id: peptide-delivery-001
front: What are the main challenges of oral peptide drug delivery?
back: "1. **Acid degradation** – gastric pH (1.5–3.5) hydrolyzes peptide bonds\n2. **Proteolytic enzymes** – pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin cleave peptides\n3. **Poor permeability** – large MW (>500 Da) limits paracellular/transcellular transport\n4. **First-pass metabolism** – hepatic degradation before reaching systemic circulation\n5. **Mucus barrier** – traps and clears peptide molecules\n6. **Efflux pumps** – P-glycoprotein expels absorbed peptides back into lumen"
tags: [peptide, oral-delivery, bioavailability, enzymes]
difficulty: beginner

id: peptide-delivery-002
front: Name three strategies to protect peptides from gastric acid degradation.
back: "1. **Enteric coatings** – pH-sensitive polymers (Eudragit L/S) dissolve only at pH >5.5\n2. **Acid-stable analogs** – replace L-amino acids with D-amino acids at cleavage sites\n3. **Encapsulation in nanoparticles/liposomes** – physical barrier shields peptide from acid\n4. **Coadministration of antacids** – buffer gastric pH temporarily\n5. **Site-specific release** – trigger release only in intestine"
tags: [gastric-protection, oral-delivery, enteric-coating, nanoparticles]
difficulty: beginner

front: What is the primary absorption site for peptides in the GI tract and why?
back: "The **jejunum** is the primary absorption site because:\n- Largest surface area (~200 m²) with villi and microvilli\n- Thinner mucus layer than stomach\n- Lower protease activity than duodenum\n- Higher pH reduces acid-catalyzed degradation\n- More favorable transcellular transport conditions\n- Paracellular tight junctions slightly more permeable than ileum"

id: peptide-delivery-005
front: How do enteric coatings protect peptide drugs?
back: "**Enteric coatings** are pH-sensitive polymers that remain insoluble in acidic gastric fluid (pH 1–4) but dissolve in the neutral/alkaline intestinal environment (pH 5.5–7.5).\n\nCommon polymers:\n- **Eudragit L100** – dissolves at pH >6.0\n- **Eudragit S100** – dissolves at pH >7.0\n- **HPMCAS** – dissolves at pH >5.5\n- **Cellulose acetate phthalate (CAP)**\n\nThey prevent gastric degradation and trigger intestinal release where absorption occurs."
tags: [enteric-coatings, oral-delivery, gastric-protection, polymers]

id: peptide-delivery-022
front: What are microspheres and how do they achieve sustained peptide release?
back: "**Microspheres** are spherical particles (1–1000 µm) with peptide dispersed or encapsulated in polymer matrix.\n\n**Sustained release mechanisms:**\n1. **Diffusion** – peptide diffuses through polymer pores\n2. **Erosion** – polymer degrades, releasing entrapped drug\n3. **Combination** – diffusion + erosion\n\n**Common polymers:** PLGA, PLA, PCL, albumin\n\n**Manufacturing:**\n- Double emulsion (W/O/W) – hydrophilic peptides\n- Spray drying\n- Coacervation\n\n**Examples:**\n- Lupron Depot (leuprolide PLGA microspheres) – 1–6 month release\n- Risperdal Consta (risperidone) – 2 week release\n\n**Challenges:** burst release, cold chain, injection volume"

front: How does albumin fusion extend peptide drug half-life?
