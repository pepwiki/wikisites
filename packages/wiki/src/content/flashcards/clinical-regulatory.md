id: fc-phase-i-trials
front: "What are the primary objectives of Phase I clinical trials?"
back: "Phase I trials evaluate safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics in healthy volunteers (20-100 subjects). They determine maximum tolerated dose (MTD), dose-limiting toxicities, and drug absorption/metabolism/excretion. Duration is typically several months."
tags: ["phase-I", "safety", "pharmacokinetics", "dose-escalation"]
difficulty: "beginner"

id: fc-phase-ii-trials
front: "What is the purpose of Phase II clinical trials?"
back: "Phase II trials assess therapeutic efficacy and optimal dosing in patients with the target disease (100-300 subjects). They establish proof-of-concept, refine dose-response relationships, and monitor short-term adverse effects. Often randomized and controlled."
tags: ["phase-II", "efficacy", "dose-finding", "proof-of-concept"]
difficulty: "beginner"

id: fc-phase-iii-trials
front: "What distinguishes Phase III clinical trials from earlier phases?"
back: "Phase III trials confirm efficacy and safety in large patient populations (1000-3000+) across multiple centers. They provide definitive evidence for regulatory approval, compare to standard of care, and identify rare adverse events. Typically randomized, double-blind, and controlled."
tags: ["phase-III", "pivotal", "registration", "multi-center"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-blinding
front: "What is blinding (masking) in clinical trials and what are its types?"
back: "Blinding prevents knowledge of treatment assignment to reduce bias. Single-blind: participant unaware. Double-blind: participant and investigator unaware. Triple-blind: participant, investigator, and analyst unaware. Open-label trials have no blinding."
tags: ["blinding", "masking", "bias", "study-design"]

back: "Placebo controls are acceptable when: no proven effective treatment exists, withholding treatment poses no serious harm, or with careful methodological justification when effective treatments exist (Declaration of Helsinki). Must have provisions for rescue therapy if needed."

Secondary endpoints provide additional information (e.g., quality of life, biomarkers). Surrogate endpoints substitute for clinical endpoints when direct measurement is impractical."

id: fc-statistical-significance
front: "What is statistical significance and how is it determined in clinical trials?"
back: "Statistical significance indicates results unlikely due to chance alone. Typically determined by p-value < 0.05 or 95% confidence interval not crossing null value. Power (usually 80-90%) is the probability of detecting a true effect. Must consider clinical significance alongside statistical significance."
tags: ["statistics", "p-value", "confidence-interval", "power-analysis"]

id: fc-bioequivalence
front: "What is bioequivalence and how is it demonstrated?"
back: "Bioequivalence shows that a generic drug delivers the same amount of active ingredient at the same rate as the reference product. Demonstrated through pharmacokinetic studies comparing AUC and Cmax. Acceptance criteria: 90% confidence interval for the ratio of means within 80-125%."
tags: ["bioequivalence", "generics", "pharmacokinetics", "AUC", "Cmax"]
