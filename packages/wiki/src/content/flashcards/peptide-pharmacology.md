id: fc-peptide-adme
front: "What are the ADME properties of peptides?"
back: "Absorption: poor oral, better parenteral. Distribution: limited Vd, mostly extracellular. Metabolism: rapid proteolysis by peptidases. Excretion: renal filtration for small peptides, hepatic for larger ones."
tags: ["pharmacology", "ADME", "pharmacokinetics"]

id: fc-peptide-bioavailability
front: "What is typical oral bioavailability for peptide drugs?"
back: "Less than 1-2% for most unmodified peptides. Poor bioavailability results from: 1) acid degradation in stomach, 2) enzymatic degradation by GI proteases, 3) poor intestinal permeability due to size and charge, 4) first-pass hepatic metabolism."
tags: ["pharmacology", "bioavailability", "oral-delivery"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-peptide-half-life
front: "What determines peptide half-life in vivo?"
back: "Half-life depends on: 1) susceptibility to proteases (primary factor), 2) renal clearance rate (molecules <60 kDa filterable), 3) protein binding, 4) tissue distribution. Most native peptides: minutes; modified peptides: hours to days."
tags: ["pharmacology", "half-life", "metabolism"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-peptide-vd
front: "What is volume of distribution (Vd) for peptides?"
back: "Vd is typically small (0.1-0.3 L/kg) because peptides are hydrophilic and largely restricted to extracellular fluid. Lipophilic modifications can increase Vd. Low Vd means peptides distribute mainly in plasma and interstitial fluid."
tags: ["pharmacology", "volume-of-distribution", "pharmacokinetics"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-peptide-clearance
front: "How are peptides cleared from the body?"
back: "Two main routes: 1) Renal clearance - glomerular filtration for peptides <60 kDa, followed by tubular reabsorption/degradation. 2) Hepatic clearance - uptake by hepatocytes and biliary excretion for larger peptides. Proteolysis in blood also contributes."
tags: ["pharmacology", "clearance", "excretion"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-first-pass-metabolism
front: "What is first-pass metabolism and why does it affect peptides?"
back: "Metabolism of a drug by the liver before it reaches systemic circulation after oral administration. Peptides are extensively degraded by hepatic peptidases and GI enzymes, contributing to their very low oral bioavailability."
tags: ["pharmacology", "first-pass-metabolism", "bioavailability"]

id: fc-oral-delivery-challenges
front: "What are the main challenges of oral peptide delivery?"
back: "1) Acid degradation in stomach (pH 1-3). 2) Enzymatic degradation (pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidases). 3) Poor mucus permeation. 4) Low intestinal epithelial permeability (MW >500 Da barrier). 5) First-pass hepatic metabolism."
tags: ["drug-delivery", "oral-delivery", "challenges"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-subcutaneous-delivery
front: "Why is subcutaneous (SC) delivery preferred for peptide drugs?"
back: "SC injection offers: 1) self-administration capability, 2) sustained absorption from depot, 3) avoids first-pass metabolism, 4) lower immunogenicity risk vs IV, 5) patient-friendly. Absorption limited by lymphatic uptake and local proteolysis."
tags: ["drug-delivery", "subcutaneous", "injection"]

id: fc-iv-delivery
front: "What are advantages and disadvantages of IV peptide delivery?"
back: "Advantages: 100% bioavailability, precise dosing, immediate effect. Disadvantages: requires healthcare professional, infection risk, no depot effect (short duration), anaphylaxis risk, higher cost. Used for acute settings or hospital-administered peptides."

id: fc-nasal-delivery
front: "How does nasal delivery work for peptide drugs?"
back: "Nasal mucosa offers rich vasculature, thin epithelium, and avoids first-pass metabolism. Challenges: limited dose volume (~0.1 mL per nostril), mucociliary clearance (15-20 min), variable absorption. Permeation enhancers (chitosan, cyclodextrins) often needed."
tags: ["drug-delivery", "nasal", "mucosal"]

back: "Large alveolar surface area (~100 m²), thin epithelium (0.1-0.2 µm), rich blood supply, avoids first-pass metabolism. Challenges: device coordination, particle size optimization (1-5 µm), lung clearance mechanisms, potential local toxicity."

id: fc-pegylation-detail
front: "How does PEGylation improve peptide pharmacokinetics?"
back: "PEGylation (attaching polyethylene glycol chains) increases hydrodynamic radius, reducing renal filtration. It shields from proteolysis, decreases immunogenicity, and extends half-life 5-100x. Trade-off: may reduce receptor binding affinity and bioactivity."
tags: ["pharmacology", "PEGylation", "drug-modification"]

id: fc-prodrug-strategy
front: "What is the prodrug strategy for peptide drugs?"
back: "A prodrug is an inactive precursor converted to active drug in vivo. For peptides: 1) lipidation to enhance membrane permeation, 2) masking charged groups with esters, 3) cyclization to resist proteolysis, 4) D-amino acid substitution at cleavage sites."
tags: ["pharmacology", "prodrug", "drug-design"]

back: "Co-administration of protease inhibitors (e.g., aprotinin, bestatin, amastatin) prevents peptide degradation at absorption sites. Used in oral formulations to protect against GI enzymes. Also, endogenous peptidase inhibitors (e.g., alpha-1-antitrypsin) protect circulating peptides."

id: fc-receptor-binding-detail
front: "How do peptides bind to their receptors?"
back: "Peptides bind cell-surface receptors (GPCRs, RTKs, ion channels) via: 1) electrostatic interactions (charged residues), 2) hydrogen bonds, 3) hydrophobic contacts, 4) van der Waals forces. Binding affinity (Kd) determines potency. Peptides cannot cross cell membranes."

id: fc-therapeutic-index
front: "What is the therapeutic index and why is it important for peptides?"
back: "TI = TD50/ED50 (toxic dose / effective dose). Higher TI = wider safety margin. Peptides generally have favorable TI because they are rapidly degraded and have specific receptor targets, reducing off-target toxicity. Exceptions: insulin (hypoglycemia risk)."
tags: ["pharmacology", "therapeutic-index", "safety"]
