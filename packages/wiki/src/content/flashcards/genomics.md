id: fc-gene-expression
front: "What is gene expression?"
back: "The process by which information from a gene is used to synthesize a functional gene product (typically a protein). Involves two main steps: transcription (DNA → mRNA) and translation (mRNA → protein). Gene expression is regulated at multiple levels including chromatin structure, transcription, post-transcriptional processing, translation, and post-translational modification. Differential gene expression allows cells with identical genomes to have different phenotypes."

id: fc-mrna-translation
front: "What is mRNA translation and how does it work?"
back: "The process by which ribosomes decode mRNA nucleotide sequences into polypeptide chains. Initiation: small ribosomal subunit binds mRNA at the 5' cap, scans for AUG start codon, initiator tRNA (Met-tRNAi) binds, large subunit joins. Elongation: aminoacyl-tRNAs enter the A site, peptide bonds form via peptidyl transferase, ribosome translocates. Termination: release factors recognize stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA), polypeptide is released. The genetic code is degenerate (64 codons for 20 amino acids), nearly universal, and read in triplets."

id: fc-protein-synthesis
front: "What are the key steps in protein synthesis?"
back: "1) Amino acid activation: aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases charge tRNAs with correct amino acids using ATP. 2) Initiation: mRNA-ribosome complex formation with initiator tRNA. 3) Elongation: sequential addition of amino acids at ~15-20 residues/second in E. coli. 4) Termination and ribosome recycling. 5) Co-translational folding begins as polypeptide emerges from ribosome exit tunnel. 6) Post-translational modifications may occur. Proteins may also require chaperone assistance for proper folding."

id: fc-post-translational-modifications
front: "What are the major types of post-translational modifications (PTMs)?"
back: "1) Phosphorylation: addition of phosphate groups by kinases (Ser, Thr, Tyr) - regulates signaling. 2) Glycosylation: N-linked (Asn) or O-linked (Ser/Thr) sugar attachment. 3) Ubiquitination: targets proteins for degradation or signaling. 4) Acetylation: Lys modification affects transcription. 5) Methylation: Arg/Lys modification. 6) SUMOylation: regulates nuclear transport. 7) Lipidation: membrane anchoring (myristoylation, palmitoylation, prenylation). 8) Proteolytic cleavage: signal peptide removal, zymogen activation. 9) Disulfide bond formation: stabilizes extracellular proteins."

id: fc-protein-folding-diseases
front: "What are protein misfolding diseases and what are some examples?"
back: "Protein misfolding diseases (proteinopathies) result from proteins adopting incorrect 3D conformations, forming toxic aggregates. Examples: Alzheimer's disease (amyloid-β plaques, tau tangles), Parkinson's disease (α-synuclein Lewy bodies), Huntington's disease (polyglutamine expansion in huntingtin), prion diseases (PrPSc misfolding, CJD, BSE), type 2 diabetes (islet amyloid polypeptide/IAPP), ALS (SOD1, TDP-43, FUS aggregation). Common mechanism: β-sheet-rich amyloid fibril formation. Toxicity may arise from oligomeric intermediates rather than mature fibrils."

id: fc-proteostasis
front: "What is proteostasis and why is it important?"
back: "Proteostasis (protein homeostasis) is the balance between protein synthesis, folding, trafficking, and degradation. Maintained by the proteostasis network including: chaperones, the ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS), autophagy-lysosome pathway, and the unfolded protein response (UPR). Critical for cellular function - proteostasis decline is a hallmark of aging. Age-related collapse of proteostasis contributes to neurodegenerative diseases. Cells have limited capacity (~30% of newly synthesized proteins are degraded by proteasome). Therapeutic strategies aim to boost proteostasis capacity."
tags: ["proteomics", "proteostasis", "chaperones", "protein-degradation", "aging"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-protein-quality-control
front: "What are the main protein quality control (PQC) mechanisms?"
back: "1) Chaperone-assisted folding: Hsp70, Hsp90, chaperonins prevent aggregation. 2) ER quality control: calnexin/calreticulin cycle for glycoproteins, ERAD (ER-associated degradation) for terminally misfolded proteins. 3) Ubiquitin-proteasome system: E1-E2-E3 ligase cascade tags misfolded proteins with K48-linked ubiquitin chains for proteasomal degradation. 4) Aggresome formation: misfolded proteins sequestered via HDAC6 and dynein motor. 5) Autophagy: clears aggregates and damaged organelles. 6) Ribosome-associated quality control (RQC): resolves stalled ribosomes."

id: fc-unfolded-protein-response
front: "What is the unfolded protein response (UPR)?"
back: "UPR is a cellular stress response activated when unfolded/misfolded proteins accumulate in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). Three main branches: 1) IRE1α: endoribonuclease splices XBP1 mRNA → active transcription factor for ER chaperone genes. 2) PERK: phosphorylates eIF2α → global translation attenuation + selective ATF4 translation. 3) ATF6: translocates to Golgi, cleaved by S1P/S2P → active transcription factor. UPR initially promotes cell survival (↑chaperones, ↑ERAD, ↓translation). Chronic/irreversible ER stress triggers apoptosis via CHOP. UPR is implicated in cancer, diabetes, and neurodegeneration."
tags: ["proteomics", "UPR", "ER-stress", "IRE1", "PERK", "ATF6"]

id: fc-heat-shock-proteins
front: "What are heat shock proteins (HSPs) and their functions?"
back: "HSPs are molecular chaperones upregulated by heat stress and other proteotoxic stresses. Major families: Hsp100 (disaggregases: ClpB/Hsp104), Hsp90 (signal transduction, hormone receptors, kinases), Hsp70/DnaK (protein folding, refolding, translocation), Hsp60/GroEL (chaperonin - folding in barrel), Hsp40/DnaJ (co-chaperone for Hsp70), small HSPs (holdases prevent aggregation). Regulation: HSF1 (heat shock factor 1) trimerizes, translocates to nucleus, binds HSE (heat shock elements) in promoters. HSPs are overexpressed in cancer - therapeutic targets."

id: fc-chaperone-proteins
front: "What are the main types of chaperone proteins?"
back: "1) Hsp70 system: Hsp70 + Hsp40 (J-domain co-chaperone) + nucleotide exchange factor (NEF). ATP-dependent cycle: binds exposed hydrophobic regions, prevents aggregation. 2) Chaperonins: Group I (GroEL-GroES in bacteria, TRiC/CCT in eukaryotes) - Anfinsen cage provides isolated folding chamber. Group II (thermosome, vault). 3) Hsp90: specialized for signaling proteins, uses co-chaptins (Hop, p23, Cdc37). 4) Small HSPs: ATP-independent holdases. 5) Trigger factor: ribosome-associated in bacteria. 6) Disaggregases: Hsp104 (yeast), Hsp110/Hsp70/Hsp40 (mammals)."

id: fc-protein-degradation
front: "What are the main pathways for protein degradation?"
back: "1) Ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS): E1-E2-E3 cascade ubiquitinates substrates → 26S proteasome degrades. Targets short-lived, misfolded, regulatory proteins. ~80% of intracellular protein degradation. 2) Autophagy: macroautophagy (bulk), microautophagy, chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA). Lysosomal degradation of long-lived proteins, aggregates, organelles. 3) Lysosomal: endocytosed membrane/extracellular proteins. 4) Calpains: calcium-dependent cytoplasmic proteases. 5) Caspases: apoptotic cleavage. 6) Mitochondrial: Lon and ClpXP proteases."

id: fc-proteasome-function
front: "How does the 26S proteasome function?"
back: "26S proteasome = 20S core particle + 19S regulatory particle (one or both ends). 20S core: four stacked heptameric rings (α7-β7-β7-α7). β1 (caspase-like), β2 (trypsin-like), β5 (chymotrypsin-like) active sites inside barrel. 19S cap: lid (deubiquitinases: Rpn11, Usp14, Uch37) and base (6 AAA+ ATPases: Rpt1-6 unfold substrates, Rpn10/Rpn13 recognize ubiquitin). Process: ubiquitin chain recognition → deubiquitination → substrate unfolding → translocation → degradation into 3-25 residue peptides → peptides further degraded to amino acids. Immunoproteasome (β1i, β2i, β5i) generates MHC class I epitopes."
tags: ["proteomics", "proteasome", "26S-proteasome", "protein-degradation", "ubiquitin"]

id: fc-autophagy
front: "What is autophagy and what are its types?"
back: "Autophagy (self-eating) is a lysosomal degradation pathway for cytoplasmic contents. Types: 1) Macroautophagy: double-membrane autophagosomes engulf cytoplasm → fuse with lysosomes. Initiated by ULK1 complex, nucleation by Beclin-1/VPS34, elongation by ATG conjugation systems (ATG5-12, LC3 lipidation). Selective forms: mitophagy (PINK1/Parkin), pexophagy, ER-phagy, ribophagy. 2) Microautophagy: lysosomal membrane invagination. 3) Chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA): Hsc70 recognizes KFERQ-like motif → LAMP-2A translocation into lysosome. Autophagy is induced by starvation, rapamycin (mTOR inhibition), and is neuroprotective."

id: fc-protein-engineering
front: "What are the main approaches to protein engineering?"
back: "Rational design: site-directed mutagenesis based on structural/functional knowledge, computational design (Rosetta). Directed evolution: random mutagenesis (error-prone PCR), recombination (DNA shuffling), selection/screening. Semi-rational: focused libraries at key positions. De novo design: computational protein design (AlphaFold, RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN). Domain swapping: combining domains from different proteins. Consensus design: using sequence conservation. Ancestral sequence reconstruction. Key applications: therapeutics (antibody engineering), industrial enzymes, biosensors, nanomaterials."

id: fc-directed-evolution
front: "What is directed evolution of proteins?"
back: "Directed evolution mimics natural selection in the laboratory to optimize protein properties. Key steps: 1) Diversification: create mutant library (error-prone PCR: ~1-5 mutations/kb, DNA shuffling: recombine homologous genes, saturation mutagenesis: systematic substitution at positions). 2) Selection/screening: phage display, yeast display, ribosome display, FACS, growth selection. 3) Iteration: repeat cycles. Landmark: Frances Arnold won 2018 Nobel Prize. Applications: enzyme activity (up to 10,000-fold improvement), thermostability, substrate specificity, enantioselectivity. Limitations: library size constraints, screening throughput."

id: fc-crispr
front: "What is CRISPR-Cas9 and how does it work?"
back: "CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a bacterial adaptive immune system repurposed for genome editing. Mechanism: Cas9 nuclease guided by single guide RNA (sgRNA) recognizes PAM sequence (NGG for SpCas9) and creates double-strand break (DSB) 3bp upstream. Repair: NHEJ (non-homologous end joining) → indels → knockout; HDR (homology-directed repair) with donor template → precise edits. Variants: Cas12a (Cpf1, staggered cuts, T-rich PAM), base editors (no DSB), prime editors (search-and-replace), Cas13 (RNA targeting), CRISPRi/CRISPRa (gene regulation). Applications: gene knockout, knock-in, screens, diagnostics (SHERLOCK, DETECTR)."
tags: ["genomics", "CRISPR", "genome-editing", "Cas9", "gene-therapy"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-gene-therapy
front: "What is gene therapy and what are its main approaches?"
back: "Gene therapy introduces genetic material into cells to treat or prevent disease. Approaches: 1) Gene addition: functional gene copy compensates for defective gene. 2) Gene editing: CRISPR/base editing corrects mutations. 3) Gene silencing: siRNA, ASO, or CRISPRi reduces harmful gene expression. Delivery: viral vectors (AAV - most common, lentivirus, adenovirus) and non-viral (lipid nanoparticles, electroporation, nanoparticles). Ex vivo: cells modified outside body (e.g., CAR-T). In vivo: direct administration. Successes: Luxturna (RPE65, blindness), Zolgensma (SMA), Casgevy (sickle cell, CRISPR). Challenges: immune responses, off-target effects, delivery efficiency, cost."
tags: ["genomics", "gene-therapy", "viral-vectors", "AAV", "CRISPR"]

Advantages: no genomic integration risk, rapid development, scalable manufacturing."

id: fc-sirna
front: "What is siRNA and how does it function therapeutically?"
back: "Small interfering RNA (siRNA) is a 21-23 nucleotide double-stranded RNA that silences gene expression via RNA interference (RNAi). Mechanism: Dicer processes dsRNA → siRNA duplex loaded into RISC (RNA-induced silencing complex) → passenger strand discarded → guide strand directs Argonaute2 to complementary mRNA → endonucleolytic cleavage → mRNA degradation. Design rules: thermodynamic asymmetry, GC content 30-52%, avoid seed region off-targets. Chemical modifications: 2'-O-methyl, 2'-F, phosphorothioate backbone, GalNAc conjugation (liver targeting). Approved: patisiran (Onpattro, LNP), givosiran, lumasiran, inclisiran (GalNAc-siRNA)."
tags: ["genomics", "siRNA", "RNAi", "RISC", "gene-silencing"]

id: fc-antisense-oligonucleotides
front: "What are antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs)?"
back: "ASOs are short (12-25 nucleotide) single-stranded DNA/RNA analogs that bind complementary mRNA via Watson-Crick base pairing. Mechanisms: 1) RNase H-dependent: DNA-like gapmer ASOs recruit RNase H1 to cleave target mRNA. 2) Steric blockade: block ribosome scanning (translation inhibition), splice site modulation (exon skipping/inclusion), or miRNA inhibition (antimiR). Chemical modifications: phosphorothioate (PS) backbone (nuclease resistance), 2'-MOE, 2'-LNA, 2'-F, morpholino, peptide nucleic acid (PNA). Approved drugs: nusinersen (Spinraza, SMA), eteplirsen (DMD exon skipping), tofersen (SOD1-ALS), volanesorsen."

id: fc-aptamers
front: "What are aptamers and how are they developed?"
back: "Aptamers are short single-stranded DNA or RNA oligonucleotides that fold into specific 3D structures and bind targets with high affinity and specificity (Kd in nM-pM range). Developed via SELEX (Systematic Evolution of Ligands by Exponential Enrichment): 1) Large random library (10^13-10^15 sequences). 2) Incubation with target. 3) Partition bound from unbound. 4) Amplification. 5) Repeat 6-15 cycles. Modifications: 2'-F, 2'-NH2 pyrimidines, PEGylation, cholesterol conjugation. Advantages: chemically synthesized, low immunogenicity, small size. FDA-approved: pegaptanib (Macugen, anti-VEGF for AMD). Applications: therapeutics, diagnostics, biosensors, targeted drug delivery."

id: fc-ribozymes
front: "What are ribozymes?"
back: "Ribozymes are catalytic RNA molecules that accelerate biochemical reactions. Natural examples: self-splicing introns (Group I, Group II), RNase P (tRNA processing), hammerhead ribozyme, hepatitis delta virus (HDV) ribozyme, glmS ribozyme (riboswitch). Mechanism: typically metal-ion dependent, cleave phosphodiester bonds. Key discovery: Thomas Cech and Sidney Altman won 1989 Nobel Prize for discovering catalytic RNA. Therapeutic applications: hammerhead ribozymes for HIV (ozogamicin), hepatitis B. Challenges: stability, delivery, in vivo activity. Connection to origin of life: RNA world hypothesis - RNA served as both genetic material and catalyst before proteins."

id: fc-epigenetics
front: "What is epigenetics?"
back: "Epigenetics is the study of heritable changes in gene expression without alterations to the DNA sequence. Main mechanisms: 1) DNA methylation: 5-methylcytosine (5mC) at CpG islands - generally silences gene expression. DNMTs add, TET enzymes remove (via 5hmC). 2) Histone modifications: acetylation (H3K27ac - active), methylation (H3K4me3 - active, H3K27me3 - repressive), phosphorylation, ubiquitination. Histone code hypothesis. 3) Chromatin remodeling: SWI/SNF, ISWI, CHD, INO80 complexes. 4) Non-coding RNAs: lncRNAs (XIST for X-inactivation), miRNAs, piRNAs. 5) 3D genome organization: TADs, compartments. Epigenetic changes are reversible - drug targets (HDAC inhibitors, DNMT inhibitors, EZH2 inhibitors)."
tags: ["genomics", "epigenetics", "DNA-methylation", "histone-modifications", "chromatin"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-transcriptomics
front: "What is transcriptomics?"
back: "Transcriptomics is the comprehensive study of all RNA transcripts (transcriptome) in a cell or tissue. Methods: 1) RNA-seq: next-generation sequencing of cDNA - gold standard for gene expression profiling. Bulk RNA-seq, single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq: 10x Genomics, Drop-seq), spatial transcriptomics (Visium, MERFISH, Slide-seq). 2) Microarrays: older hybridization-based method. 3) Long-read: PacBio Iso-Seq, Oxford Nanopore - full-length isoform detection. 4) Nascent RNA: GRO-seq, NET-seq, TT-seq - measures transcriptionally active RNA. Applications: differential expression, alternative splicing, cell type identification, developmental trajectories, disease biomarkers. Analysis: STAR/HISAT2 (alignment), DESeq2/edgeR (differential expression), Seurat/Scanpy (single-cell)."
tags: ["genomics", "transcriptomics", "RNA-seq", "single-cell", "gene-expression"]

id: fc-metabolomics
front: "What is metabolomics?"
back: "Metabolomics is the comprehensive study of small molecule metabolites (<1500 Da) in biological systems. Approaches: 1) Untargeted: unbiased profiling of all detectable metabolites. 2) Targeted: quantification of specific metabolite classes. Analytical platforms: mass spectrometry (LC-MS, GC-MS, CE-MS) and NMR spectroscopy. Sample types: blood, urine, tissue, cell culture media. Key concepts: metabolome (all metabolites), flux analysis (13C-labeled substrates), metabolic fingerprinting. Data analysis: PCA, PLS-DA, pathway enrichment (MetaboAnalyst), spectral databases (HMDB, METLIN, MassBank). Applications: biomarker discovery, drug metabolism, disease diagnosis (inborn errors of metabolism), microbiome-host interactions, precision nutrition."
