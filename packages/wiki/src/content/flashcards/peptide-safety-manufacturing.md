front: "What are the clinical signs of peptide-induced anaphylaxis?"
back: "Anaphylaxis signs include: (1) skin - urticaria, flushing, angioedema, (2) respiratory - bronchospasm, laryngeal edema, dyspnea, (3) cardiovascular - hypotension, tachycardia, shock, (4) GI - nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, (5) neurological - dizziness, syncope, altered consciousness. Onset typically within minutes to 1 hour of administration. Biphasic reactions can occur 4-12 hours later."

id: fc-safety-anaphylaxis-treatment
front: "What is the first-line treatment for peptide-induced anaphylaxis?"
back: "First-line treatment: IM epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg (1:1000) in mid-outer thigh for adults, 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.3 mg) for children. Repeat every 5-15 minutes if needed. Adjuncts: IV fluids, oxygen, bronchodilators, antihistamines (H1 + H2 blockers), corticosteroids. Position patient supine with legs elevated. Monitor for biphasic reaction for 4-24 hours."

id: fc-safety-immunogenicity-definition
front: "What is immunogenicity in the context of peptide therapeutics?"
back: "Immunogenicity is the ability of a therapeutic peptide to trigger an immune response, producing anti-drug antibodies (ADAs). Types: (1) binding ADAs - bind drug without neutralizing, (2) neutralizing ADAs - block pharmacological activity, (3) cross-reactive ADAs - bind endogenous proteins. Consequences include loss of efficacy, altered pharmacokinetics, immune complex disease, and anaphylaxis."

id: fc-safety-lyophilization-process
front: "What is lyophilization and why is it used for peptide drugs?"
back: "Lyophilization (freeze-drying) removes water by: (1) freezing the solution, (2) primary drying - sublimation under vacuum, (3) secondary drying - desorption of bound water. Benefits: extended stability (2-3 years vs months for liquid), reduced aggregation, easier transport. Challenges: reconstitution time, potential for incomplete dissolution, cryoconcentration effects. Excipients (mannitol, sucrose) protect structure during freezing."

id: fc-safety-deamidation
front: "What is deamidation and which amino acids are susceptible?"
back: "Deamidation is the conversion of asparagine (Asn) to aspartate (Asp) or isoaspartate (isoAsp), and glutamine (Glu) to glutamate, releasing ammonia. Asn-Gly sequences are most susceptible due to minimal steric hindrance. Factors accelerating deamidation: high pH (>7), elevated temperature, prolonged storage. Deamidation can alter charge, conformation, receptor binding, and immunogenicity."
