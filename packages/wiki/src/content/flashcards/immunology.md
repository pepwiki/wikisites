back: "The process by which cells display processed antigen fragments on their surface bound to MHC molecules, enabling T cells to recognize and respond to pathogens or abnormal cells."

id: fc-mhc-class-I
front: "What types of antigens does MHC class I present and to which cells?"
back: "MHC class I presents endogenous (intracellular) peptides (8-10 amino acids) to CD8+ cytotoxic T cells. Found on all nucleated cells."
tags: ["immunology", "MHC", "MHC-class-I", "antigen-presentation"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-mhc-class-II
front: "What types of antigens does MHC class II present and to which cells?"
back: "MHC class II presents exogenous (extracellular) peptides (13-25 amino acids) to CD4+ helper T cells. Found on professional antigen-presenting cells (dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells)."
tags: ["immunology", "MHC", "MHC-class-II", "antigen-presentation"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-t-cell-receptor
front: "What is a T cell receptor (TCR)?"
back: "A heterodimeric protein (usually alpha-beta chains) on T cells that recognizes peptide-MHC complexes. Unlike antibodies, TCRs are membrane-bound and recognize processed peptides, not free antigens."
tags: ["immunology", "TCR", "T-cells", "receptors"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

---

id: fc-b-cell-receptor
front: "What is a B cell receptor (BCR)?"
back: "A membrane-bound immunoglobulin on B cells that recognizes native (unprocessed) antigens. Upon activation, B cells differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies with the same specificity."
tags: ["immunology", "BCR", "B-cells", "receptors"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-antibody-structure
front: "What is the basic structure of an antibody?"
back: "Y-shaped molecule with 2 heavy chains and 2 light chains. Contains: Fab region (antigen-binding with variable domains VH/VL) and Fc region (constant, mediates effector functions like complement activation and phagocyte binding)."
tags: ["immunology", "antibodies", "structure", "immunoglobulin"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-epitope-definition
front: "What is an epitope?"
back: "The specific portion of an antigen (antigenic determinant) that is recognized and bound by an antibody or T cell receptor. Can be 5-15 amino acids for T cell epitopes or larger surface areas for B cell epitopes."
tags: ["immunology", "epitopes", "antigens"]
difficulty: "beginner"

---

---

id: fc-linear-epitope
front: "What is a linear (continuous) epitope?"
back: "An epitope consisting of a continuous sequence of amino acids in a protein's primary structure. Can be recognized by both antibodies and T cells even when the protein is denatured."
tags: ["immunology", "epitopes", "linear-epitope"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

---

id: fc-conformational-epitope
front: "What is a conformational (discontinuous) epitope?"
back: "An epitope formed by amino acids that are distant in primary sequence but brought together by protein folding. Recognized by antibodies in native protein structure. Destroyed by denaturation."
tags: ["immunology", "epitopes", "conformational-epitope"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-peptide-vaccine
front: "What is a peptide vaccine?"
back: "A vaccine composed of specific antigenic peptides (epitopes) that elicit targeted immune responses. Advantages include safety, stability, and ability to focus immunity on conserved epitopes. Often require adjuvants and delivery systems."
tags: ["immunology", "peptides", "vaccines", "peptide-vaccine"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-tumor-associated-antigen
front: "What are tumor-associated antigens (TAAs)?"
back: "Proteins overexpressed or abnormally expressed on tumor cells but also present on normal cells. Examples: HER2 (breast cancer), MAGE (melanoma), CEA (colorectal cancer). Used as targets for cancer immunotherapy."
tags: ["immunology", "cancer", "TAA", "tumor-antigens"]
difficulty: "advanced"

---

id: fc-neoantigen
front: "What are neoantigens?"
back: "Novel peptides arising from tumor-specific mutations (somatic mutations) that are presented by MHC molecules. Not found on normal cells, making them ideal targets for personalized cancer immunotherapy with minimal autoimmune risk."
tags: ["immunology", "cancer", "neoantigens", "personalized-medicine"]
difficulty: "advanced"

id: fc-immune-checkpoint-inhibitor
front: "What are immune checkpoint inhibitors?"
back: "Drugs that block inhibitory immune checkpoints (molecular 'brakes'), unleashing T cells to attack tumors. Examples: anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4 antibodies. Revolutionized cancer treatment."
tags: ["immunology", "cancer", "checkpoint-inhibitors", "immunotherapy"]
difficulty: "advanced"

---

---

id: fc-pd1-pdl1
front: "What is the PD-1/PD-L1 axis?"
back: "PD-1 (on T cells) binds PD-L1 (on tumor/normal cells), delivering an inhibitory signal that suppresses T cell function. Tumors exploit this to evade immunity. Blocking this axis with antibodies (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) reactivates anti-tumor T cells."
tags: ["immunology", "PD-1", "PD-L1", "checkpoint", "cancer"]
difficulty: "advanced"

---

id: fc-ctla4
front: "What is CTLA-4 and its function?"
back: "Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte Associated protein 4 - an inhibitory receptor on T cells that competes with CD28 for B7 ligands, dampening T cell activation. Ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) blocks this, enhancing anti-tumor immunity."
tags: ["immunology", "CTLA-4", "checkpoint", "T-cells", "cancer"]
difficulty: "advanced"

back: "A major class of cytokines (IL-1 through IL-40+) that mediate communication between leukocytes. Key examples: IL-2 (T cell growth), IL-4 (B cell differentiation), IL-6 (acute phase response), IL-10 (anti-inflammatory)."
tags: ["immunology", "cytokines", "interleukins"]

id: fc-interferons
front: "What are interferons and their main types?"
back: "Cytokines that 'interfere' with viral replication. Types: Type I (IFN-alpha, IFN-beta - antiviral, produced by most cells), Type II (IFN-gamma - macrophage activation, produced by T/NK cells), Type III (IFN-lambda - mucosal antiviral)."
tags: ["immunology", "cytokines", "interferons", "antiviral"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

tags: ["immunology", "cytokines", "TNF-alpha", "inflammation"]

id: fc-complement-system
front: "What is the complement system?"
back: "A cascade of ~30 serum proteins that enhances (complements) immune defense. Three activation pathways (classical, lectin, alternative) converging on C3 convertase. Functions: opsonization, membrane attack complex (MAC), inflammation (C3a/C5a)."
tags: ["immunology", "complement", "innate-immunity"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

---

id: fc-innate-immunity
front: "What is innate immunity?"
back: "The immediate, non-specific first line of defense. Components: physical barriers (skin, mucus), cellular (macrophages, neutrophils, NK cells), soluble (complement, cytokines). No immunological memory. Activated within hours."
tags: ["immunology", "innate-immunity", "defense"]
difficulty: "beginner"

---

id: fc-adaptive-immunity
front: "What is adaptive immunity?"
back: "The specific, slower immune response mediated by T and B lymphocytes. Characterized by: antigen specificity (TCR/BCR), diversity, immunological memory, and self/non-self discrimination. Takes days to develop on first exposure."
tags: ["immunology", "adaptive-immunity", "lymphocytes"]
difficulty: "beginner"

---

---

id: fc-immunological-memory
front: "What is immunological memory?"
back: "The ability of the adaptive immune system to mount a faster, stronger response upon re-encounter with the same antigen. Mediated by memory T and B cells. Basis for vaccination. Memory cells persist for decades."
tags: ["immunology", "memory", "adaptive-immunity", "vaccination"]
difficulty: "beginner"

id: fc-tolerance
front: "What is immunological tolerance?"
back: "The immune system's state of unresponsiveness to specific antigens. Central tolerance (thymic deletion of self-reactive T cells) and peripheral tolerance (regulatory T cells, anergy, deletion). Prevents autoimmunity."
tags: ["immunology", "tolerance", "autoimmunity", "self-tolerance"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-autoimmunity
front: "What is autoimmunity?"
back: "Immune-mediated attack against self-antigens due to loss of tolerance. Examples: Type 1 diabetes (pancreatic beta cells), rheumatoid arthritis (joints), multiple sclerosis (myelin), lupus (DNA/nuclear antigens)."
tags: ["immunology", "autoimmunity", "autoimmune-disease", "tolerance"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

back: "An inappropriate immune response to normally harmless environmental antigens (allergens) such as pollen, dust mites, or food proteins. Mediated primarily by IgE antibodies and mast cell degranulation (Type I hypersensitivity)."
tags: ["immunology", "allergy", "IgE", "hypersensitivity"]

back: "Type I (IgE-mediated, immediate - allergy), Type II (IgG/IgM against cell-surface antigens - transfusion reactions), Type III (immune complex deposition - serum sickness), Type IV (T cell-mediated, delayed - contact dermatitis, TB test)."
tags: ["immunology", "hypersensitivity", "allergy", "immune-response"]

id: fc-immunodeficiency
front: "What is immunodeficiency?"
back: "A state where the immune system's ability to fight infection is compromised. Primary (genetic, e.g., SCID, DiGeorge syndrome) or secondary (acquired, e.g., HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy, malnutrition). Results in increased susceptibility to infections."
tags: ["immunology", "immunodeficiency", "infection", "immune-system"]
difficulty: "intermediate"

id: fc-transplant-immunology
front: "What are the main immunological challenges in transplantation?"
back: "Allorecognition: recipient T cells recognize donor MHC molecules as foreign. Hyperacute rejection (pre-formed antibodies), acute rejection (T cell-mediated), chronic rejection (antibody/immune complex). Managed with immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine)."
tags: ["immunology", "transplantation", "rejection", "MHC", "immunosuppression"]
difficulty: "advanced"
