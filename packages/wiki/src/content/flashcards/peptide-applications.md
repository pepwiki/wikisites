id: peptide-biomarkers
front: What are peptide biomarkers?
back: Peptides whose presence, absence, or concentration in biological fluids indicates a disease state, physiological condition, or treatment response. Examples include BNP for heart failure, amyloid-beta peptides for Alzheimer's disease, and procalcitonin for bacterial infection.
tags: [peptides, biomarkers, diagnostics, clinical]

id: diagnostic-peptides
front: How are peptides used in diagnostics?
back: Peptides serve as capture agents or targets in immunoassays, biosensors, and imaging probes. They can detect antibodies, bind disease-specific receptors, or act as substrates for disease-associated enzymes. Their small size allows rapid diffusion, high tissue penetration, and low immunogenicity compared to antibodies.
tags: [peptides, diagnostics, biosensors, immunoassays]
difficulty: beginner

id: therapeutic-peptides
front: What are therapeutic peptides and why are they important?
back: Therapeutic peptides are peptide-based drugs used to treat diseases. Over 80 peptide drugs are approved globally, including insulin, GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide), and vasopressin analogs. They offer high potency and selectivity with generally fewer off-target effects than small molecules, though bioavailability and half-life remain challenges.
tags: [peptides, therapeutics, drug-development, pharmacology]
difficulty: beginner

id: peptide-vaccines
front: What are peptide vaccines?
back: Vaccines composed of synthetic peptide epitopes that stimulate immune responses against specific antigens. They present defined B-cell or T-cell epitopes from pathogens or tumor antigens, avoiding whole-protein risks. Advantages include precise targeting and safety; challenges include weak immunogenicity often requiring adjuvants or delivery systems.
tags: [peptides, vaccines, immunology, epitopes]
difficulty: intermediate

id: antimicrobial-peptides
front: What are antimicrobial peptides (AMPs)?
back: Short cationic and amphipathic peptides (typically 12–50 amino acids) that kill bacteria, fungi, and viruses by disrupting microbial membranes or intracellular targets. Examples include defensins, cathelicidins, and magainins. They are part of innate immunity and are being developed as alternatives to conventional antibiotics.
tags: [peptides, antimicrobial, AMPs, innate-immunity, infection]
difficulty: beginner

id: anti-cancer-peptides
front: How do anti-cancer peptides work?
back: Anti-cancer peptides exert anticancer effects through multiple mechanisms: direct tumor cell killing via membrane disruption, induction of apoptosis, inhibition of angiogenesis, immune modulation, or targeted delivery of cytotoxic payloads. Examples include LTX-315 and tumor-homing peptides. They often show selectivity for cancer cells due to differences in membrane composition.
tags: [peptides, oncology, anti-cancer, apoptosis]

id: peptide-drug-conjugates
front: What are peptide-drug conjugates (PDCs)?
back: Molecules consisting of a peptide (targeting moiety) linked to a cytotoxic or therapeutic payload via a cleavable linker. The peptide directs the drug to specific receptors on target cells, improving selectivity and reducing systemic toxicity. Examples include AN-152 (doxorubicin conjugated to an LHRH analog) and BT-1718.
tags: [peptides, drug-conjugates, targeted-therapy, oncology]
difficulty: advanced

They bind disease-specific receptors with high affinity, enabling visualization of tumors, inflammation, or infection. Examples include RGD peptides for integrin imaging in tumors and fluorescence-guided surgery using IRDye 800CW-labeled peptides.

id: peptide-hydrogels
front: What are peptide hydrogels?
back: Self-assembling peptides that form hydrogel networks through non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, electrostatic forces). They are used as scaffolds for tissue engineering, drug delivery vehicles, wound dressings, and 3D cell culture matrices. Examples include RADA16 and EAK16 peptides.

id: peptide-nanomaterials
front: What are peptide nanomaterials?
back: Nanostructures formed by self-assembly of peptides into nanofibers, nanotubes, nanospheres, or nanosheets. They have applications in drug delivery, tissue engineering, catalysis, and electronics. Notable examples include diphenylalanine (FF) nanotubes, amyloid-inspired fibrils, and peptide amphiphile nanofibers developed by the Stupp lab.

They are widely used as dietary supplements for joint health, skin elasticity, and bone density. Bioactive collagen peptides may stimulate endogenous collagen synthesis, reduce inflammation, and support cartilage repair.

id: carnosine
front: What is carnosine and what are its functions?
back: A dipeptide (beta-alanyl-L-histidine) found in high concentrations in muscle and brain tissue. It acts as an antioxidant, buffers intracellular pH, chelates metal ions, and may prevent protein glycation and cross-linking. It is studied for anti-aging, neuroprotective, and exercise performance applications.
tags: [peptides, carnosine, antioxidant, anti-aging, muscle]

id: glutathione
front: What is glutathione and why is it important?
back: A tripeptide (gamma-glutamyl-cysteinyl-glycine) and the body's primary intracellular antioxidant. It neutralizes reactive oxygen species, detoxifies xenobiotics via conjugation, maintains redox balance, and supports immune function. Depletion is linked to oxidative stress, aging, and numerous diseases. Supplementation via oral, IV, or liposomal forms is increasingly popular.
tags: [peptides, glutathione, antioxidant, detoxification, redox]

id: defensins
front: What are defensins?
back: Small cationic antimicrobial peptides (3–5 kDa) with six conserved cysteine residues forming three disulfide bonds. Two main types: alpha-defensins (found in neutrophils and Paneth cells) and beta-defensins (produced by epithelial cells). They kill microbes by membrane permeabilization and also modulate immune responses. Human beta-defensin 2 and 3 are particularly important in mucosal defense.
tags: [peptides, defensins, antimicrobial, innate-immunity, epithelial]
difficulty: intermediate

id: cathelicidins
front: What are cathelicidins?
back: A family of antimicrobial peptides characterized by a conserved N-terminal cathelin domain and a variable C-terminal antimicrobial domain. In humans, the sole cathelicidin is LL-37, processed from the hCAP18 precursor. LL-37 has broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity and roles in immune modulation, wound healing, chemotaxis, and angiogenesis. Vitamin D upregulates its expression.
tags: [peptides, cathelicidins, LL-37, antimicrobial, innate-immunity]

id: magainins
front: What are magainins?
back: Antimicrobial peptides first isolated from the skin of the African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis) by Michael Zasloff in 1987. Magainin 1 and 2 are 23-amino acid alpha-helical peptides that form pores in bacterial membranes. They were among the first AMPs characterized and inspired extensive research into peptide antibiotics. MSI-78 (pexiganan) is a magainin derivative that reached Phase III clinical trials.

id: cell-penetrating-peptides
front: What are cell-penetrating peptides (CPPs)?
back: Short peptides (typically 5–30 amino acids) that facilitate cellular uptake of attached cargo through direct membrane translocation or endocytic pathways. They are cationic or amphipathic and can deliver proteins, nucleic acids, nanoparticles, and small molecules into cells. Examples include TAT, penetratin, polyarginine sequences, and transportan.
tags: [peptides, CPPs, drug-delivery, membrane-translocation]

id: tat-peptide
front: What is the TAT peptide?
back: A cell-penetrating peptide derived from the trans-activator of transcription protein of HIV-1. The minimal sequence GRKKRRQRRRPQ (residues 47–57) mediates rapid cellular uptake. It has been extensively used to deliver cargo molecules including proteins, nanoparticles, and imaging agents into cells. It enters through multiple endocytic and direct translocation mechanisms.

id: penetratin
front: What is penetratin?
back: A 16-amino acid cell-penetrating peptide derived from the Drosophila Antennapedia homeodomain protein. Its sequence RQIKIWFQNRRMKWKK enables it to cross biological membranes efficiently. It was one of the first discovered CPPs (1994) and is used for intracellular delivery of bioactive molecules. It penetrates via direct translocation and endocytosis.

id: rgd-peptides
front: What are RGD peptides?
back: Peptides containing the Arg-Gly-Asp (RGD) tripeptide motif that binds integrin receptors, particularly αvβ3 and αvβ5 overexpressed on tumor vasculature and cancer cells. They are used for tumor targeting in imaging (¹⁸F-Galacto-RGD), drug delivery, and anti-angiogenesis therapy. Cyclic RGD peptides (e.g., cRGDfK) show improved affinity and selectivity.

id: ngr-peptides
front: What are NGR peptides?
back: Peptides containing the Asn-Gly-Arg (NGR) motif that selectively bind aminopeptidase N (CD13), a marker of tumor vasculature. They home to tumor neovasculature and are used for targeted delivery of cytotoxic drugs, proapoptotic peptides, and imaging agents. CNGRC variants improve stability. TNF-alpha conjugated to NGR (NGR-TNF) has been in clinical trials.
tags: [peptides, NGR, tumor-homing, CD13, targeted-therapy]

id: cyclic-peptides
front: What are cyclic peptides and their advantages?
back: Peptides with their backbone connected in a ring structure (head-to-tail, disulfide, or other cyclization). Cyclization constrains conformation, improving receptor binding affinity and selectivity, and enhances metabolic stability by protecting against exopeptidase degradation. Examples include cyclosporine A, daptomycin, and vancomycin. They access protein-protein interaction targets inaccessible to small molecules.
tags: [peptides, cyclic-peptides, cyclization, stability, drug-design]
difficulty: intermediate

id: stapled-peptides
front: What are stapled peptides?
back: Alpha-helical peptides covalently constrained by a hydrocarbon staple formed via olefin metathesis of side-chain-attached olefinic amino acids. The staple stabilizes the alpha-helical conformation, improving protease resistance, cell permeability, and target binding affinity. They are used to inhibit protein-protein interactions. ALRN-6924 (MDM2/MDMX inhibitor) is an example in clinical trials.
tags: [peptides, stapled-peptides, alpha-helix, PPI-inhibitors, drug-design]
difficulty: advanced
