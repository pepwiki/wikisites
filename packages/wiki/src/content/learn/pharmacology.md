# Therapeutic Peptides

## Introduction

Peptide therapeutics represent a rapidly growing class of drugs. They offer high specificity and potency with fewer side effects compared to small molecules. As of 2026, over 80 peptide drugs are FDA-approved, with many more in development.

## Routes of Administration

### Injectable Routes

- **Intravenous (IV)**: Immediate effect, 100% bioavailability
- **Subcutaneous (SC)**: Most common for peptide drugs
- **Intramuscular (IM)**: Slower absorption than SC
- **Intrathecal (IT)**: Direct CNS delivery

### Non-Intravenous Routes

- **Intranasal (IN)**: Nasal delivery for CNS-targeted peptides
- **Oral**: Limited by GI degradation (semaglutide uses SNAC)
- **Transdermal**: Patch delivery (limited availability)
- **Pulmonary**: Inhalation delivery (investigational)

## Half-Life Challenges

Peptide drugs typically have short half-lives due to:

- **Enzymatic degradation**: Proteases in blood and tissues
- **Renal clearance**: filtration and excretion by kidneys
- **Receptor-mediated clearance**: internalization and degradation

### Half-Life Enhancement Strategies

| Strategy                  | Mechanism                        | Example                  |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| PEGylation                | Add polyethylene glycol          | PEGylated interferons    |
| Albumin binding           | Extend circulatory half-life     | Semaglutide (fatty acid) |
| D-amino acid substitution | Resist protease degradation      | Desmopressin             |
| Cyclization               | Increase stability               | Octreotide               |
| depot formulations        | Slow release from injection site | Leuprolide microspheres  |

## Mechanisms of Action

### Receptor Agonism

- Bind to and activate receptors
- Mimic natural peptide hormones
- Examples: Insulin, GLP-1 agonists

### Receptor Antagonism

- Bind to receptors without activation
- Block natural peptide effects
- Examples: GnRH antagonists

### Enzyme Inhibition

- Inhibit specific enzymes
- Prevent peptide degradation
- Examples: Protease inhibitors

### Carrier-Mediated Transport

- Bind to carriers for cellular uptake
- Deliver payloads to specific cells
- Examples: Antibody-drug conjugates

## Advantages of Peptide Drugs

1. **High specificity**: Selective for target receptors
2. **High potency**: Effective at low doses
3. **Low toxicity**: Few off-target effects
4. **Biodegradable**: Metabolized to amino acids
5. **Predictable metabolism**: Well-understood degradation pathways

## Limitations of Peptide Drugs

1. **Short half-life**: Require frequent dosing
2. **Poor oral bioavailability**: GI degradation
3. **Immunogenicity**: Potential antibody formation
4. **Manufacturing cost**: Complex synthesis
5. **Stability**: Susceptible to aggregation

## Emerging Trends

### Oral Peptide Drugs

- Semaglutide (Rybelsus) with SNAC absorption enhancer
- Research into other oral delivery technologies

### Long-Acting Formulations

- Depot injections (monthly or longer)
- Continuous delivery systems

### Peptide-Drug Conjugates

- Peptides linked to cytotoxic agents
- Targeted cancer therapy

### Multifunctional Peptides

- Dual or triple receptor agonism
- Combination therapies in single molecule

## Clinical Development Pipeline

The peptide drug pipeline includes over 150 candidates in clinical trials, addressing:

- Metabolic diseases (diabetes, obesity)
- Oncology (various cancers)
- Cardiovascular diseases
- Neurological disorders
- Rare diseases

## Summary

Peptide therapeutics offer unique advantages in specificity and potency. While challenges remain in half-life and delivery, innovative strategies are expanding the clinical utility of peptide drugs across multiple therapeutic areas.
