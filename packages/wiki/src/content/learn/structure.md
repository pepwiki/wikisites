# Protein Structure Levels

## Introduction

Proteins have a hierarchical structure organized into four levels: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary. Each level is stabilized by specific forces and interactions. Understanding these levels is fundamental to predicting protein function and designing therapeutic peptides.

## Primary Structure

The **primary structure** is the linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain. It is determined by the genetic code and represents the most basic level of protein organization.

### Key Features

- Linear sequence of amino acids connected by peptide bonds
- Determines all higher levels of structure
- Encoded in DNA sequence
- Read from N-terminus to C-terminus

### Stabilizing Forces

- **Peptide bonds**: Covalent bonds between amino acids
- **Disulfide bonds**: Covalent bonds between cysteine residues (can be considered part of primary structure)

### Importance

The primary structure dictates:

- Folding pathway
- Final three-dimensional structure
- Biological function
- Disease susceptibility (e.g., sickle cell anemia from single amino acid change)

## Secondary Structure

**Secondary structure** refers to local folding patterns of the polypeptide backbone. The two major types are alpha-helices and beta-sheets.

### Alpha-Helix (α-helix)

- Right-handed coil structure
- 3.6 residues per turn
- Rise of 1.54 Å per residue
- Hydrogen bonds between residues i and i+4
- Stabilized by backbone hydrogen bonds

### Beta-Sheet (β-sheet)

- Extended polypeptide chains
- Hydrogen bonds between adjacent strands
- Can be parallel or antiparallel
- Side chains alternate above and below the sheet
- Pleated appearance

### Other Secondary Structures

- **3₁₀ helix**: 3 residues per turn, tighter than α-helix
- **π-helix**: 4.1 residues per turn, wider than α-helix
- **Beta-turns**: Connect β-strands, often contain proline
- **Random coil**: No regular repeating structure

### Stabilizing Forces

- **Hydrogen bonds**: Between backbone N-H and C=O groups
- **Dipole interactions**: Alignment of peptide bond dipoles

### Ramachandran Plot

Secondary structures occupy specific regions on the Ramachandran plot:

- α-helix: φ ≈ -57°, ψ ≈ -47°
- β-sheet: φ ≈ -120° to -140°, ψ ≈ +110° to +140°

## Tertiary Structure

**Tertiary structure** is the overall three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain. It results from interactions between amino acid side chains.

### Stabilizing Forces

| Force Type                 | Description                                      | Strength      |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------- |
| Hydrophobic interactions   | Nonpolar side chains cluster in protein interior | Moderate      |
| Hydrogen bonds             | Between polar side chains                        | Weak-Moderate |
| Ionic bonds (salt bridges) | Between charged side chains                      | Weak          |
| Disulfide bonds            | Covalent bonds between cysteines                 | Strong        |
| Van der Waals forces       | Weak attractions between all atoms               | Weak          |

### Common Tertiary Structure Motifs

- **Alpha-helical bundles**: Groups of α-helices packed together
- **Beta-barrels**: β-sheets forming closed structures
- **Alpha/beta proteins**: Mixed α-helix and β-sheet regions
- **Metal coordination**: Metal ions stabilizing structure

### Folding Pathways

Proteins fold through specific pathways:

1. Secondary structure formation
2. Hydrophobic collapse
3. Tertiary contact formation
4. Final structure optimization

## Quaternary Structure

**Quaternary structure** is the arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits) into a functional protein complex.

### Key Features

- Involves two or more polypeptide chains
- Chains can be identical (homo-oligomers) or different (hetero-oligomers)
- Stabilized by same forces as tertiary structure
- Essential for function of many proteins

### Examples

- **Hemoglobin**: Tetramer (α₂β₂) for oxygen transport
- **DNA polymerase**: Multiple subunits for replication
- **Antibodies**: Four chains (2 heavy, 2 light) for immune function

### Quaternary Structure Levels

- **Dimer**: Two subunits
- **Trimer**: Three subunits
- **Tetramer**: Four subunits
- **Hexamer**: Six subunits
- **Oligomer**: Multiple subunits (2-12)
- **Polymer**: Many repeating subunits

## Structural Stability

### Thermodynamic Considerations

Protein stability depends on the balance between:

- **Enthalpy (ΔH)**: Favorable interactions (H-bonds, ionic bonds)
- **Entropy (ΔS)**: Conformational freedom
- **Gibbs free energy (ΔG)**: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

Most proteins are marginally stable (ΔG ≈ 20-60 kJ/mol).

### Denaturation

Proteins can be denatured by:

- Heat (thermal denaturation)
- pH changes (acid/base denaturation)
- Chemical denaturants (urea, guanidinium chloride)
- Mechanical stress

## Structure-Function Relationship

Protein structure directly determines function:

- **Enzyme active sites**: Specific 3D arrangement of catalytic residues
- **Binding sites**: Complementary shape for ligand binding
- **Structural proteins**: Mechanical properties from structure
- **Signaling proteins**: Conformational changes for signal transduction

## Practical Applications

Understanding protein structure is essential for:

- **Drug design**: Targeting specific structural features
- **Protein engineering**: Modifying structure for desired function
- **Disease understanding**: Misfolding diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's)
- **Biotechnology**: Designing industrial enzymes

## Summary

The four levels of protein structure form a hierarchical organization where each level influences the next. Primary structure determines all higher levels, while quaternary structure enables complex biological functions through subunit interactions.
