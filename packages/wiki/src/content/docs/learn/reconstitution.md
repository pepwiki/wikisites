## Introduction

Most research peptides are shipped as **lyophilized (freeze-dried) powders** to ensure long-term stability. Lyophilization removes water by freezing the peptide solution and applying a vacuum, allowing ice to sublimate directly from solid to vapor. This process preserves peptide integrity by preventing hydrolysis and microbial growth during storage and transport.

Before use, peptides must be **reconstituted** — dissolved in an appropriate solvent to create a working solution. Proper reconstitution technique is essential for maintaining peptide activity and ensuring accurate dosing.

## Bacteriostatic Water

**Bacteriostatic water** (BAC water) is sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative. It is the preferred solvent for peptide reconstitution because:

- The benzyl alcohol inhibits bacterial growth, extending shelf life of reconstituted solutions
- It is isotonic and compatible with most peptides
- It provides a slightly acidic to neutral pH environment

**Why not plain sterile water?** Regular sterile water lacks preservatives and can support bacterial growth once the vial is opened. Additionally, plain water may have a slightly different pH that could affect peptide stability. Bacteriostatic water is inexpensive and widely available, making it the standard choice.

## Step-by-Step Reconstitution

### Calculate the Required Volume

Before beginning, calculate the volume needed to achieve your desired concentration:

```
Volume (mL) = Peptide mass (mg) / Desired concentration (mg/mL)
```

For example, to reconstitute a 5 mg vial to 2.5 mg/mL:
```
Volume = 5 mg / 2.5 mg/mL = 2 mL
```

Common reconstitution volumes for research peptides:

| Vial Mass | Target Concentration | Volume to Add |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| 1 mg | 1 mg/mL | 1 mL |
| 5 mg | 2 mg/mL | 2.5 mL |
| 10 mg | 2 mg/mL | 5 mL |
| 25 mg | 2.5 mg/mL | 10 mL |

### Preparation

1. Gather supplies: peptide vial, bacteriostatic water, sterile syringe (3 mL or 5 mL), alcohol swabs, clean work surface
2. Wash hands thoroughly and put on clean gloves
3. Wipe down work surface with alcohol
4. Inspect the peptide vial for damage or discoloration
5. Swab the rubber stopper of both the peptide vial and the BAC water vial with alcohol swabs; allow to air dry

### Injection Technique

1. Draw the calculated volume of bacteriostatic water into the syringe
2. Remove the cap from the peptide vial (do not touch the exposed stopper)
3. Insert the needle through the center of the rubber stopper
4. **Slowly inject the water down the inside wall of the vial** — do not spray directly onto the powder, as this can cause foaming and peptide denaturation
5. Withdraw the needle and recap

### Mixing

**Gently swirl the vial** between your palms to mix. The powder should dissolve within 1-2 minutes.

**NEVER shake the vial vigorously.** Shaking introduces air bubbles that can:
- Denature or aggregate the peptide
- Cause foaming that makes accurate dosing difficult
- Reduce peptide activity

If the powder does not dissolve immediately, gently tilt the vial back and forth. Some peptides may require 5-10 minutes for complete dissolution. If particulate matter remains visible after 15 minutes of gentle mixing, the peptide may be degraded.

## Storage After Reconstitution

- Store reconstituted peptides at **2-8°C** (refrigerated, not frozen)
- Keep the vial upright to minimize contact with the rubber stopper
- Label the vial with peptide name, concentration, reconstitution date, and expiration
- Use within **2-4 weeks** for optimal stability
- Do not freeze reconstituted solutions unless specifically indicated, as freeze-thaw cycles can damage peptides

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Injecting water directly onto powder | Foaming, aggregation, denaturation | Inject slowly down vial wall |
| Shaking the vial | Air bubbles, peptide damage | Swirl gently between palms |
| Using non-sterile water | Microbial contamination | Use bacteriostatic water only |
| Reconstituting too far in advance | Degradation, reduced activity | Use within 2-4 weeks |
| Using incorrect volume | Wrong concentration, dosing errors | Calculate carefully before starting |

## Safety Considerations

- **Clean technique is essential.** Always swab vial tops and work on a clean surface
- **Use proper sharps disposal.** Never recap needles; dispose of used syringes in an approved sharps container
- **Monitor for adverse reactions** if the peptide is being used in research applications
- **Document everything.** Record reconstitution date, concentration, lot number, and expiration for traceability

## Troubleshooting

**Cloudy solution:** May indicate incomplete dissolution or precipitation. Allow additional time for gentle mixing. If cloudiness persists, the peptide may be degraded or contaminated.

**Color change:** Some peptides naturally have color (e.g., those containing Trp or Tyr). Unexpected color changes may indicate oxidation or degradation.

**Precipitation:** Visible particles after reconstitution suggest degradation or poor solubility. Do not use — prepare a fresh vial.
