# Peptide Biochemistry: Scientifically Rigorous Quiz

## Advanced Structural & Post-Translational Biochemistry

### Question 1
**Topic:** Peptide Bond Resonance Energy

The peptide bond exhibits partial double-bond character due to resonance delocalization of the nitrogen lone pair into the carbonyl π-system. What is the approximate resonance stabilization energy of the peptide bond?

- A) 40 kJ/mol
- B) 60 kJ/mol
- C) 80 kJ/mol
- D) 120 kJ/mol

**Correct Answer:** C) 80 kJ/mol

**Explanation:** The peptide bond resonance energy is approximately 80 kJ/mol, arising from delocalization of the nitrogen lone pair electrons into the C=O π* orbital. This gives the C–N bond ~40% double-bond character (bond length 1.33 Å vs. 1.47 Å for a pure C–N single bond). The rotational barrier is ~88 kJ/mol, restricting ω rotation to ±5° from planarity. The dipole moment of the peptide bond is approximately 3.5 D, with partial charges δ⁺ on carbon and δ⁻ on oxygen.

### Question 2
**Topic:** Cis-Trans Isomerization of Xaa-Pro Bonds

The cis-trans isomerization of Xaa-Pro peptide bonds is catalyzed by peptidyl-prolyl cis-trans isomerases (PPIases). What is the equilibrium constant (Kt) for the trans:cis ratio of Xaa-Pro bonds at 25°C, and which enzyme class catalyzes this interconversion?

- A) Kt ≈ 4.0; catalyzed by cyclophilins (EC 5.2.1.8)
- B) Kt ≈ 4.0; catalyzed by FK506-binding proteins (EC 5.2.1.8)
- C) Kt ≈ 0.4; catalyzed by parvulin-type PPIases (EC 5.2.1.8)
- D) Both A and B are correct

**Correct Answer:** D) Both A and B are correct

**Explanation:** Xaa-Pro bonds have Kt (trans:cis) ≈ 4.0 at 25°C, compared to Kt ≈ 1000 for non-proline residues. Three PPIase families catalyze this isomerization: cyclophilins (Cyps, inhibited by cyclosporin A), FK506-binding proteins (FKBPs, inhibited by FK506/rapamycin), and parvulins (including Pin1). All are classified under EC 5.2.1.8. The activation energy for uncatalyzed isomerization is ~80 kJ/mol; enzymes reduce this to ~40 kJ/mol. Pin1 specifically recognizes pSer/pThr-Pro motifs in the trans configuration and catalyzes cis-trans interconversion, regulating cell cycle progression.

### Question 3
**Topic:** Ramachandran Plot Allowed Regions

In the Ramachandran plot (φ/ψ map), which combination of dihedral angles corresponds to the right-handed α-helix conformation?

- A) φ = -57°, ψ = -47°
- B) φ = -139°, ψ = +135°
- C) φ = -57°, ψ = +135°
- D) φ = -119°, ψ = +113°

**Correct Answer:** A) φ = -57°, ψ = -47°

**Explanation:** The Ramachandran plot maps sterically allowed φ (Cᵢ₋₁–Nᵢ–Cαᵢ–Cᵢ) and ψ (Nᵢ–Cαᵢ–Cᵢ–Nᵢ₊₁) torsion angles. The αR region (right-handed α-helix) is centered at φ = -57°, ψ = -47°. The β-sheet region spans φ ≈ -139°, ψ ≈ +135° (antiparallel) and φ ≈ -119°, ψ ≈ +113° (parallel). The αL region (left-handed helix) is at φ ≈ +57°, ψ ≈ +47°, primarily occupied by glycine due to its absent Cβ sterics. Allowed regions are defined by van der Waals radii: rN = 1.65 Å, rCα = 1.75 Å, rC = 1.65 Å, rO = 1.40 Å.

### Question 4
**Topic:** Alpha Helix Parameters

What are the precise structural parameters of the α-helix?

- A) 3.6 residues/turn, 0.54 nm pitch, 0.15 nm rise per residue
- B) 3.0 residues/turn, 0.60 nm pitch, 0.20 nm rise per residue
- C) 4.4 residues/turn, 0.54 nm pitch, 0.12 nm rise per residue
- D) 2.4 residues/turn, 0.54 nm pitch, 0.23 nm rise per residue

**Correct Answer:** D) 3.6 residues/turn, 0.54 nm pitch, 0.15 nm rise per residue

**Explanation:** The right-handed α-helix (Pauling & Corey, 1951) has: 3.6 residues per turn, pitch (P) = 0.54 nm (5.4 Å), rise per residue (d) = 0.15 nm (1.5 Å), and rise per turn = 0.54 nm. Hydrogen bonds form between C=O of residue i and N–H of residue i+4, with N⋯O distance = 2.98 Å and N–H⋯O angle ≈ 160°. The φ/ψ angles are approximately -57°/-47°. Each residue rotates the helix by 100° (360°/3.6). The helix dipole moment is ~0.5 D per residue, with partial positive charge at the N-terminus and partial negative charge at the C-terminus.

### Question 5
**Topic:** Beta Sheet Hydrogen Bonding Patterns

What is the inter-strand hydrogen bond distance (N⋯O) in β-sheets, and how does it differ between parallel and antiparallel arrangements?

- A) 0.28 nm (parallel) vs. 0.30 nm (antiparallel)
- B) 0.347 nm spacing, with antiparallel sheets having more linear H-bonds
- C) 0.35 nm spacing, with parallel sheets having more linear H-bonds
- D) 0.347 nm spacing, with both having identical H-bond geometry

**Correct Answer:** B) 0.347 nm spacing, with antiparallel sheets having more linear H-bonds

**Explanation:** β-sheets have inter-strand spacing of 0.347 nm (3.47 Å) for the Cα⋯Cα distance. In antiparallel sheets, hydrogen bonds are nearly perpendicular to the strand direction (N⋯O = 2.80 Å, N–H⋯O angle ≈ 170°), creating more linear, stronger H-bonds. In parallel sheets, H-bonds are inclined ~60° to the strand axis (N⋯O = 2.96 Å, N–H⋯O angle ≈ 150°), with alternating short and long H-bond pairs. The twist angle between strands is typically 0-30°. Each residue has φ ≈ -139°, ψ ≈ +135° (antiparallel) or φ ≈ -119°, ψ ≈ +113° (parallel).

### Question 6
**Topic:** Collagen Triple Helix

What are the characteristic structural features of the collagen triple helix?

- A) Gly-X-Y repeats, 3.0 residues/turn, 0.94 nm pitch
- B) Gly-X-Y repeats, 3.6 residues/turn, 0.54 nm pitch
- C) Ala-X-Y repeats, 3.0 residues/turn, 0.94 nm pitch
- D) Gly-X-Y repeats, 3.0 residues/turn, 0.86 nm pitch

**Correct Answer:** A) Gly-X-Y repeats, 3.0 residues/turn, 0.94 nm pitch

**Explanation:** Collagen forms a left-handed polyproline II-type helix with Gly-X-Y repeating triplets (X often Pro, Y often 4-hydroxyproline). Parameters: 3.0 residues per turn, pitch = 0.94 nm (9.4 Å), rise per residue = 0.31 nm (3.1 Å). Three chains wind into a right-handed triple helix (superhelix) with C2 symmetry. Glycine is mandatory at every third position due to steric constraints at the triple helix axis. Hydroxylation of Pro (by prolyl 4-hydroxylase, EC 1.14.11.2) stabilizes the triple helix through stereoelectronic effects (gauche effect on Cγ–O bond) and water-mediated hydrogen bonds. Tm of collagen ≈ 42°C; hydroxyproline-deficient collagen has Tm ≈ 24°C.

### Question 7
**Topic:** Disulfide Bond Formation (Ero1/PDI Pathway)

In the endoplasmic reticulum, disulfide bonds are formed through a thiol-disulfide relay system. What is the correct electron transfer pathway?

- A) Ero1α → PDI → substrate protein
- B) PDI → Ero1α → substrate protein
- C) GSH → GSSG → PDI → substrate protein
- D) Ero1α → glutathione → PDI → substrate protein

**Correct Answer:** A) Ero1α → PDI → substrate protein

**Explanation:** In the ER lumen, Ero1α (endoplasmic reticulum oxidoreductin 1, EC 1.8.4.-) is the primary oxidase that generates disulfide bonds using FAD as cofactor, transferring electrons to O₂ to produce H₂O₂. Ero1α contains a CxxC catalytic motif (Cys391-Cys394 in human) and a regulatory CxxC pair (Cys94-Cys99). Ero1α oxidizes PDI (protein disulfide-isomerase, EC 5.3.4.1), which then transfers disulfide bonds to substrate proteins via thiol-disulfide exchange. PDI contains thioredoxin-like domains (a, a', b, b', c) with CGHC active sites in the a and a' domains. The redox potential of PDI (E°' ≈ -180 mV) is optimized for both oxidation and isomerization.

### Question 8
**Topic:** Protein Folding Thermodynamics (Anfinsen's Dogma)

Anfinsen's thermodynamic hypothesis states that the native structure is determined by the amino acid sequence. What is the typical free energy difference (ΔG) between the native and unfolded states of a globular protein?

- A) -5 to -15 kJ/mol
- B) -20 to -60 kJ/mol
- C) -100 to -200 kJ/mol
- D) -500 to -1000 kJ/mol

**Correct Answer:** B) -20 to -60 kJ/mol

**Explanation:** The marginal stability of proteins (ΔGN-U ≈ -20 to -60 kJ/mol at 25°C, pH 7) results from a delicate balance: the folding free energy is the difference between large opposing terms (ΔH ≈ -500 to -1000 kJ/mol from van der Waals, H-bonds, electrostatics; TΔS ≈ -400 to -900 kJ/mol from conformational entropy loss). The hydrophobic effect contributes ~60% of stability (ΔGhydrophobic ≈ 25 cal/mol per Å² of buried nonpolar surface). Anfinsen's ribonuclease A refolding experiments (1961) demonstrated that the thermodynamically most stable state is kinetically accessible. Exceptions include: prions (kinetic traps), amyloid fibrils (metastable aggregates), and proteins requiring chaperones for kinetic partitioning.

### Question 9
**Topic:** Levinthal's Paradox and Folding Funnels

Levinthal's paradox states that a protein cannot fold by random conformational search. For a 100-residue protein with 3 rotameric states per residue, how many possible conformations exist?

- A) 10³
- B) 10¹⁰
- C) 10⁴⁸
- D) 10¹⁰⁰

**Correct Answer:** C) 10⁴⁸

**Explanation:** With n residues and 3 rotameric states per residue, the conformational space is 3ⁿ. For n = 100: 3¹⁰⁰ ≈ 5.15 × 10⁴⁷ ≈ 10⁴⁸. If sampling each conformation takes 10⁻¹³ s (one vibrational period), exhaustive search would require ~10³⁵ seconds, exceeding the age of the universe (~10¹⁷ s). The folding funnel model (Dill, 1985; Wolynes, 1995) resolves this: proteins fold along a multidimensional energy landscape with a funneled shape biased toward the native state. The principle of minimal frustration (energy landscape theory) explains that natural proteins have evolved sequences where local interactions are minimally frustrated, creating a smooth funnel with few kinetic traps. Typical folding times are 1 μs to 1 s.

### Question 10
**Topic:** Chaperonin Mechanism (GroEL/GroES)

What is the correct mechanistic sequence for the GroEL/GroES chaperonin system in *E. coli*?

- A) ATP binding → GroES binding → substrate encapsulation → ATP hydrolysis → GroES release
- B) Substrate binding → ATP hydrolysis → GroES binding → substrate release → ATP binding
- C) GroES binding → ATP binding → substrate encapsulation → GroES release → ATP hydrolysis
- D) Substrate binding → ATP binding → GroES binding → ATP hydrolysis → GroES release

**Correct Answer:** D) Substrate binding → ATP binding → GroES binding → ATP hydrolysis → GroES release

**Explanation:** GroEL is a homo-oligomeric complex (14 subunits, 800 kDa) arranged as two stacked heptameric rings. Each subunit has three domains: apical (substrate/GroES binding), intermediate (hinge), and equatorial (ATP binding, EC 3.6.1.-). Mechanism: (1) Unfolded substrate binds to hydrophobic apical domains of GroEL ring (cis ring). (2) ATP binds to equatorial domains of same ring (Kd ≈ 5 μM). (3) GroES caps the cis ring (bullet-shaped asymmetric complex). (4) ATP hydrolysis (7 ATP per ring) triggers conformational change: apical domain rotates 60°, expanding cavity volume from ~85,000 Å³ to ~175,000 Å³. Hydrophobic lining converts to hydrophilic. (5) Substrate folds inside Anfinsen cage (τ ≈ 10 s). (6) ATP hydrolysis in trans ring triggers GroES release. The entire cycle takes ~15 s at 25°C.

What is the subunit composition and catalytic mechanism of the 26S proteasome?

- D) 20S core (α7β7β7α7) + 20S cap; Thr protease mechanism

**Correct Answer:** B) 20S core (α7β7β7α7) + 19S cap; Thr protease mechanism

### Question 12
**Topic:** Ubiquitin Conjugation Cascade

What is the correct order of ubiquitin transfer and the energy source for ubiquitin activation?

- A) E1 → E2 → substrate; GTP hydrolysis
- B) E1 → E2 → E3 → substrate; ATP → AMP + PPi
- C) E1 → E3 → E2 → substrate; ATP → ADP + Pi
- D) E2 → E1 → E3 → substrate; GTP → GDP + Pi

**Correct Answer:** B) E1 → E2 → E3 → substrate; ATP → AMP + PPi

**Explanation:** Ubiquitin (76 residues, 8.5 kDa) activation requires ATP → AMP + PPi (two-step adenylation, EC 6.2.1.45): (1) Ubiquitin C-terminal Gly76 adenylated (Ub-AMP intermediate). (2) E1 active site Cys attacks Ub-AMP, forming E1~Ub thioester. (3) Ubiquitin transfers to E2 active site Cys (transthiolation). (4) E3 ligases catalyze substrate ubiquitination: RING E3s (bind E2~Ub and substrate simultaneously, ~600 in humans) or HECT E3s (form E3~Ub intermediate first, ~30 in humans) or RBR E3s (hybrid mechanism). Isopeptide bond forms between Ub Gly76 C-terminus and substrate Lys ε-amino group. Chain types: K48 (proteasomal degradation), K63 (signaling), M1/linear (NF-κB), K11 (ERAD). Human genome encodes ~2 E1s, ~40 E2s, ~600 E3s.

### Question 13
**Topic:** Autophagy Pathways

What distinguishes macroautophagy from chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA) at the molecular level?

- A) Macroautophagy uses LC3-II lipidation; CMA uses Hsc70 and LAMP-2A
- B) Macroautophagy requires mTOR inhibition; CMA requires AMPK activation
- C) Macroautophagy degrades entire organelles; CMA only degrades cytosolic proteins
- D) Macroautophagy uses Atg5-Atg12 conjugation; CMA uses ubiquitin tagging

**Correct Answer:** A) Macroautophagy uses LC3-II lipidation; CMA uses Hsc70 and LAMP-2A

**Explanation:** **Macroautophagy**: Initiation by ULK1 complex (ULK1/Atg1, Atg13, FIP200, Atg101), nucleation by PI3K class III complex (VPS34/Beclin-1/Atg14L), elongation by two ubiquitin-like conjugations: Atg12-Atg5-Atg16L1 complex and LC3-II (LC3-I conjugated to PE by Atg4, Atg7 E1-like, Atg3 E2-like). Substrate-Hsc70 complex binds LAMP-2A (lysosome-associated membrane protein type 2A) receptor, which multimerizes to form translocation complex. Substrate unfolds and translocates into lysosomal lumen. CMA is constitutive but upregulated by prolonged starvation (>10 h) when macroautophagy declines.

### Question 16
**Topic:** Phosphorylation Kinases

What is the structural basis for the specificity of Ser/Thr kinases versus Tyr kinases?

- A) Ser/Thr kinases have a catalytic base (Asp); Tyr kinases use a catalytic base (Asp) but with different DFG motif
- B) Ser/Thr kinases use Mg²⁺ coordination; Tyr kinases use Mn²⁺
- C) Ser/Thr kinases have a catalytic base (Asp166 in PKA); Tyr kinases have a catalytic base (Asp810 in EGFR) with distinct P-loop conformation

**Correct Answer:** C) Ser/Thr kinases have a catalytic base (Asp166 in PKA); Tyr kinases have a catalytic base (Asp810 in EGFR) with distinct P-loop conformation

**Explanation:** GPI anchor structure: protein-CO-NH-CH₂-CH₂-PO₄-6Manα1,2Manα1,6Manα1,4GlcNα1,6-myo-inositol-1-PO₄-diacylglycerol. Biosynthesis (ER membrane, ≥10 steps): (1) GlcNAc transfer to PI by GPI-GnT complex (EC 2.4.1.-) → GlcNAc-PI. (2) De-N-acetylation → GlcN-PI. (3) Acylation of inositol (2-position) by PIG-W. (4) Transfer of three Man residues from Dol-P-Man by PIG-M, PIG-V, PIG-B. (5) Ethanolamine phosphate (EthN-P) transfer from phosphatidylethanolamine by PIG-N, PIG-G, PIG-O. (6) GPI transamidase (GPAA1/PIG-T/PIG-K/PIG-S/PIG-U complex) cleaves C-terminal GPI signal peptide and attaches GPI to new C-terminal Asp. **PI-PLC** (Bacillus cereus, EC 3.1.4.10) cleaves the phosphodiester bond between GlcN and 1-O-acyl-myo-inositol, releasing diacylglycerol and soluble protein-EthN-PO₄-Man₃GlcN-acyl-inositol. This is why PI-PLC releases GPI-anchored proteins from membranes (e.g., alkaline phosphatase, Thy-1, decay-accelerating factor/CD55). Sensitivity to PI-PLC is a diagnostic criterion for GPI-anchored proteins.
