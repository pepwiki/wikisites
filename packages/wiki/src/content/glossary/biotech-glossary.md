## Biotechnology

### Expression Vector

**Category:** Biotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** transformation, promoter, selection-marker

A DNA molecule used to introduce and express a gene in a host cell. Contains elements for replication, transcription, and translation including promoter, origin of replication, selection marker, and cloning site. Used to produce recombinant peptides and proteins in bacteria, yeast, or mammalian cells.

---

### Transformation

**Category:** Biotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** expression-vector, transfection, competent-cell

The process of introducing foreign DNA into a bacterial cell. Methods include heat shock (CaCl2 treatment) and electroporation. Transformants are identified using selection markers on the introduced plasmid.

---

### Transfection

**Category:** Biotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** transformation, expression-vector, mammalian-cell-culture

The process of introducing nucleic acids into eukaryotic cells. Methods include lipofection, electroporation, and viral transduction. Used for recombinant protein production in mammalian and insect cell systems.

---

### Selection Marker

**Category:** Biotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** expression-vector, transformation, antibiotic-resistance

A gene on an expression vector that allows identification of cells containing the vector. Common markers: antibiotic resistance genes (ampicillin, kanamycin), auxotrophic complementation (HIS3, LEU2), or fluorescent proteins.

---

### Promoter

**Category:** Biotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** expression-vector, transcription, gene-expression

A DNA sequence that initiates transcription of a gene. Controls when, where, and how much protein is produced. Types: constitutive (CMV, SV40), inducible (T7, tet-on), tissue-specific. Choice affects expression level and regulation.

---

## Bioinformatics

### Sequence Alignment

**Category:** Bioinformatics | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** homology, multiple-alignment, blast

Arrangement of two or more sequences to identify regions of similarity. Types: global (Needleman-Wunsch) and local (Smith-Waterman). Used to infer functional, structural, or evolutionary relationships between proteins.

---

### Homology

**Category:** Bioinformatics | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** sequence-alignment, evolutionary-relationship, ortholog

Similarity between sequences due to shared ancestry. Homologous proteins may have similar structure and function. Types: orthologs (different species, same gene) and paralogs (same species, gene duplication). Homology is inferred from sequence similarity but is a qualitative statement.

---

### E-Value

**Category:** Bioinformatics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** bit-score, blast, statistical-significance

The expected number of database hits with a given score or better, assuming random sequences. Lower E-values indicate more significant matches. E-value < 0.001 is typically considered significant for homology detection. Depends on database size and scoring matrix.

---

### Bit Score

**Category:** Bioinformatics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** e-value, sequence-alignment, scoring-matrix

A normalized score derived from the raw alignment score using the substitution matrix and gap penalties. Higher bit scores indicate better alignments. Independent of database size, unlike E-values. Used to compare alignment quality across different searches.

---

### Multiple Alignment

**Category:** Bioinformatics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** sequence-alignment, phylogenetic-tree, conserved-residue

Alignment of three or more sequences simultaneously. Reveals conserved regions, evolutionary patterns, and family relationships. Tools: Clustal Omega, MUSCLE, MAFFT. Used for phylogenetic analysis, primer design, and identifying functionally important residues.

---

## Materials Science

### Hydrogel

**Category:** Materials Science | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** scaffold, biomaterial, swelling

A three-dimensional network of hydrophilic polymers that can absorb large amounts of water (10-99% water content). Used for drug delivery, tissue engineering, and wound healing. Peptide-functionalized hydrogels can mimic extracellular matrix properties.

---

### Scaffold

**Category:** Materials Science | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** hydrogel, biomaterial, tissue-engineering

A three-dimensional structure that provides mechanical support and biological signals for cell attachment and tissue formation. Materials: natural (collagen, fibrin) or synthetic (PLGA, PEG). Can be loaded with peptides for cell signaling.

---

### Biomaterial

**Category:** Materials Science | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** biocompatibility, scaffold, implant

A material designed to interact with biological systems for medical purposes. Types: metals, ceramics, polymers, and composites. Must be biocompatible and meet mechanical requirements. Peptide coatings can improve biomaterial integration with host tissue.

---

### Biocompatibility

**Category:** Materials Science | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** biomaterial, cytotoxicity, immunogenicity

The ability of a material to perform with an appropriate host response in a specific application. Evaluated by cytotoxicity, sensitization, irritation, and systemic toxicity tests. Peptide modifications can enhance biocompatibility of synthetic materials.

---

### Biodegradation

**Category:** Materials Science | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** biomaterial, hydrolysis, erosion

The breakdown of materials by biological agents (enzymes, microorganisms) or chemical processes (hydrolysis, oxidation). Rate of biodegradation must match tissue regeneration rate for scaffolds. Peptide-based materials can be designed with tunable degradation rates.

---

## Nanotechnology

### Nanoparticle

**Category:** Nanotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** nanofiber, liposome, drug-delivery

A particle with at least one dimension between 1-100 nm. Types: polymeric, metallic (gold, silver), lipid, and silica. Properties differ from bulk material due to surface area-to-volume ratio and quantum effects. Used for drug delivery, imaging, and sensing.

---

### Nanofiber

**Category:** Nanotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** nanoparticle, scaffold, electrospinning

A fiber with diameter in the nanometer range (typically 10-1000 nm). Produced by electrospinning, self-assembly, or phase separation. High surface area and porosity make them ideal for tissue scaffolds, filters, and drug delivery. Peptide nanofibers can mimic extracellular matrix.

---

### Nanotube

**Category:** Nanotechnology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nanoparticle, carbon-nanotube, self-assembly

A cylindrical nanostructure. Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are the most common type. Peptide nanotubes form by self-assembly of cyclic peptides with alternating D and L amino acids. Used in drug delivery, biosensing, and as templates for nanofabrication.

---

### Quantum Dot

**Category:** Nanotechnology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nanoparticle, fluorescence, imaging

Semiconductor nanocrystals (2-10 nm) with size-dependent optical properties due to quantum confinement. Emit narrow, tunable fluorescence with high photostability. Used for multiplexed imaging and tracking. Peptide-conjugated quantum dots enable targeted cell labeling.

---

### Liposome

**Category:** Nanotechnology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** nanoparticle, drug-delivery, lipid-bilayer

A spherical vesicle composed of one or more phospholipid bilayers enclosing an aqueous core. Can encapsulate hydrophilic drugs in the core and hydrophobic drugs in the bilayer. PEGylated liposomes (stealth liposomes) have extended circulation time.

---

## Imaging

### Fluorescence

**Category:** Imaging | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** phosphorescence, fret, fluorophore

Emission of light by a molecule after absorbing photons. The emitted photon has lower energy (longer wavelength) than the absorbed photon. Fluorescent labels (FITC, rhodamine, GFP) attached to peptides enable visualization of cellular processes.

---

### Phosphorescence

**Category:** Imaging | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** fluorescence, triplet-state, luminescence

Emission of light from an excited triplet state after intersystem crossing from the singlet excited state. Longer lifetime than fluorescence (microseconds to seconds). Useful for time-gated imaging to eliminate background autofluorescence.

---

### FRET (Förster Resonance Energy Transfer)

**Category:** Imaging | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** fret, bret, donor-acceptor

Non-radiative energy transfer between two fluorophores (donor and acceptor) when in close proximity (1-10 nm). Efficiency depends on distance (1/r6). Used to measure protein-protein interactions, conformational changes, and enzyme activity in real time.

---

### BRET (Bioluminescence Resonance Energy Transfer)

**Category:** Imaging | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** fret, bioluminescence, luciferase

Energy transfer from a bioluminescent donor (luciferase) to a fluorescent acceptor. No external excitation light required, reducing background. Used to study protein-protein interactions in living cells. NanoLuc luciferase is commonly used as donor.

---

### Bioluminescence

**Category:** Imaging | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** fluorescence, luciferase, chemiluminescence

Light production by living organisms through enzyme-catalyzed reactions. Luciferase enzymes oxidize luciferin substrates to produce light. Used as a reporter gene in molecular biology and for in vivo imaging of gene expression and cell tracking.

---

## Spectroscopy

### Chemical Shift

**Category:** Spectroscopy | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nmr-spectroscopy, ppm, shielding

The resonance frequency of a nucleus relative to a reference standard, expressed in parts per million (ppm). Reflects the electronic environment around the nucleus. In peptide NMR, 1H and 13C chemical shifts provide information about secondary structure and hydrogen bonding.

---

### Coupling Constant (J-Coupling)

**Category:** Spectroscopy | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** chemical-shift, nmr-spectroscopy, spin-spin-coupling

The splitting of NMR signals due to through-bond interactions between neighboring nuclei. Measured in Hz. 3J coupling constants (Karplus equation) relate to dihedral angles and are used to determine peptide backbone conformation.

---

### NOE (Nuclear Overhauser Effect)

**Category:** Spectroscopy | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nmr-spectroscopy, noe-distance, structure-determination

Through-space dipolar coupling between nuclei that are close in space (<5 Å). NOE intensity is proportional to 1/r6, providing distance restraints for 3D structure determination. NOESY experiments are essential for NMR-based peptide structure elucidation.

---

### Relaxation

**Category:** Spectroscopy | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nmr-spectroscopy, t1, t2

The return of excited nuclear spins to equilibrium after radiofrequency excitation. T1 (spin-lattice) relaxation involves energy transfer to the environment. T2 (spin-spin) relaxation involves loss of coherence. Provides information about molecular dynamics and flexibility.

---

### Diffusion

**Category:** Spectroscopy | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** nmr-spectroscopy, diffusion-coefficient, molecular-size

The random movement of molecules in solution. Measured by diffusion-ordered spectroscopy (DOSY) in NMR. Diffusion coefficient relates to molecular size and shape through the Stokes-Einstein equation. Used to estimate peptide oligomerization state.

---

## Chromatography

### Retention Time

**Category:** Chromatography | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** selectivity, resolution, hplc

The time elapsed between injection of a sample and detection of a peak at the detector. Depends on analyte-stationary phase interactions. Used for qualitative identification when compared to standards under identical conditions.

---

### Selectivity

**Category:** Chromatography | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** retention-time, resolution, stationary-phase

The ability of a chromatographic system to distinguish between two analytes. Quantified by the selectivity factor (alpha) = ratio of retention factors. Affected by stationary phase chemistry, mobile phase composition, and temperature.

---

### Resolution

**Category:** Chromatography | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** selectivity, retention-time, peak-width

A measure of how well two adjacent peaks are separated. Rs = 2(tR2 - tR1)/(w1 + w2). Resolution >= 1.5 indicates baseline separation. Affected by efficiency (plate count), selectivity, and retention. Critical for peptide purification.

---

### Plate Count (N)

**Category:** Chromatography | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** resolution, peak-width, column-efficiency

A measure of column efficiency. N = 16(tR/w)2 or N = 5.54(tR/w0.5)2. Higher plate count means narrower peaks and better separation. Affected by particle size, column length, flow rate, and diffusion coefficients of analytes.

---

### Peak Capacity

**Category:** Chromatography | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** resolution, gradient-elution, proteomics

The maximum number of peaks that can be resolved with a specified resolution in a given time window. Increases with gradient time and column efficiency. Critical for proteomics applications where thousands of peptides must be separated.

---

## Mass Spectrometry

### m/z (Mass-to-Charge Ratio)

**Category:** Mass Spectrometry | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** charge-state, mass-spectrum, ion

The mass of an ion divided by its charge state. The fundamental measurement in mass spectrometry. For peptides, multiply-charged ions (z > 1) are common in electrospray ionization. The mass can be calculated from multiple charge states.

---

### Charge State

**Category:** Mass Spectrometry | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** m/z, electrospray-ionization, multiply-charged-ion

The number of charges (protons) carried by an ion. In electrospray ionization, peptides typically acquire multiple charges (z = 2-5+). Higher charge states produce lower m/z values. Charge state distribution reflects peptide structure and solution conditions.

---

### Isotope Pattern

**Category:** Mass Spectrometry | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** m/z, monoisotopic-peak, mass-accuracy

The distribution of peaks resulting from natural isotopic abundance (13C, 15N, 18O, 34S). For peptides, the monoisotopic peak (all light isotopes) and isotope envelope provide information about molecular formula and charge state.

---

### Fragmentation

**Category:** Mass Spectrometry | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** collision-induced-dissociation, b-ion, y-ion, tandem-ms

The breaking of chemical bonds in ions to produce smaller fragment ions. In peptide MS, fragmentation primarily occurs at peptide bonds producing b-ions (N-terminal) and y-ions (C-terminal). Fragmentation patterns reveal amino acid sequence.

---

### Collision Energy

**Category:** Mass Spectrometry | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** collision-induced-dissociation, fragmentation, normalized-collision-energy

The kinetic energy converted to internal energy during ion-molecule collisions in CID. Higher collision energy produces more extensive fragmentation. Normalized collision energy (NCE) accounts for charge state and m/z. Optimal energy balances sequence coverage and signal intensity.

---

## Thermodynamics

### Enthalpy (ΔH)

**Category:** Thermodynamics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** entropy, free-energy, calorimetry

The heat absorbed or released during a process at constant pressure. Measured by isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC). For peptide binding reactions, enthalpy reflects hydrogen bonding, van der Waals, and electrostatic interactions. Negative ΔH indicates exothermic binding.

---

### Entropy (ΔS)

**Category:** Thermodynamics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** enthalpy, free-energy, disorder

A measure of the disorder or randomness of a system. Positive ΔS increases favorability of binding (TΔS contribution). Hydrophobic interactions are entropy-driven at room temperature. Conformational entropy loss occurs when peptides adopt ordered structures upon binding.

---

### Free Energy (ΔG)

**Category:** Thermodynamics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** enthalpy, entropy, equilibrium-constant

The thermodynamic potential that determines spontaneity: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. Negative ΔG indicates a spontaneous process. Related to equilibrium constant by ΔG = -RT ln K. Binding free energy determines affinity of peptide-protein interactions.

---

### Equilibrium Constant (K)

**Category:** Thermodynamics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** free-energy, dissociation-constant, association-constant

The ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium. For binding: Ka = [AB]/[A][B]. Larger K indicates stronger binding. Related to free energy by ΔG = -RT ln K. Kd (dissociation constant) = 1/Ka is commonly used for peptide-receptor binding.

---

### Van't Hoff Analysis

**Category:** Thermodynamics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** equilibrium-constant, enthalpy, temperature-dependence

A method to determine binding enthalpy from the temperature dependence of the equilibrium constant. Plot of ln K vs 1/T gives slope = -ΔH/R. Assumption of temperature-independent ΔH may not hold for peptide-protein interactions with large heat capacity changes.

---

## Kinetics

### Rate Constant (k)

**Category:** Kinetics | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** reaction-rate, activation-energy, arrhenius

The proportionality constant between reaction rate and reactant concentrations. Units depend on reaction order (s-1 for first order, M-1s-1 for second order). For peptide-protein binding: kon (association) and koff (dissociation) rate constants determine affinity (Kd = koff/kon).

---

### Activation Energy (Ea)

**Category:** Kinetics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** arrhenius, rate-constant, transition-state

The minimum energy required for a reaction to occur. Higher activation energy means slower reaction rate at a given temperature. Enzymes lower activation energy of peptide bond hydrolysis. Measured from temperature dependence of rate constants.

---

### Arrhenius Equation

**Category:** Kinetics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** activation-energy, rate-constant, temperature-dependence

Relates rate constant to temperature: k = A·e^(-Ea/RT), where A is the pre-exponential factor and Ea is activation energy. Plot of ln k vs 1/T gives slope = -Ea/R. Used to predict reaction rates at different temperatures for peptide degradation and synthesis.

---

### Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

**Category:** Kinetics | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** catalytic-efficiency, vmax, km

A model describing enzyme-catalyzed reaction rates. v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]). Parameters: Vmax (maximum rate), Km (substrate concentration at half-Vmax). Used to characterize protease activity on peptide substrates and to compare enzyme variants.

---

### Catalytic Efficiency (kcat/Km)

**Category:** Kinetics | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** michaelis-menten, rate-constant, enzyme-kinetics

The second-order rate constant for enzyme action at low substrate concentrations. kcat/Km combines catalytic rate (kcat) and substrate binding (Km). Upper limit is diffusion-controlled (~108-109 M-1s-1). Used to evaluate protease specificity for different peptide substrates.
