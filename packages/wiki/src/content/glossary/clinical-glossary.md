## Toxicology

### LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%)

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** noael, loael, toxicity, therapeutic-index

The dose of a substance that kills 50% of a test population. Used as a measure of acute toxicity. Determined in animal studies during preclinical development. Lower LD50 values indicate higher toxicity.

---

### NOAEL (No Observed Adverse Effect Level)

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** loael, therapeutic-index, safety-margin

The highest dose at which no adverse effects are observed in a study. Used to set human starting doses for clinical trials. Determined from repeat-dose toxicity studies in two species (typically rodent and non-rodent).

---

### LOAEL (Lowest Observed Adverse Effect Level)

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** noael, toxicity, dose-response

The lowest dose at which adverse effects are observed. Always higher than or equal to NOAEL. Used when NOAEL cannot be determined. Helps establish dose-response relationships for safety assessment.

---

### Therapeutic Index

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** ld50, ed50, safety-margin

Ratio of toxic dose to therapeutic dose (TD50/ED50). A higher therapeutic index indicates a wider margin of safety. Peptide drugs generally have favorable therapeutic indices due to high target specificity and predictable clearance.

---

### Adverse Event

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** adverse-reaction, side-effect, safety-monitoring

Any untoward medical occurrence in a patient administered a pharmaceutical product, regardless of causality. Monitored throughout clinical development and post-market surveillance. Graded by severity using CTCAE criteria.

---

### Toxicity

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** ld50, noael, adverse-event

The degree to which a substance can cause damage to an organism. Types include acute (single dose), chronic (repeated dose), organ-specific (hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity), and systemic. Assessed through in vitro and in vivo studies.

---

### Genotoxicity

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** mutagenicity, carcinogenicity, ames-test

The ability of a substance to damage DNA, causing mutations or chromosomal aberrations. Assessed by Ames test, micronucleus assay, and chromosomal aberration tests. Positive genotoxicity results are a major red flag in drug development.

---

### Carcinogenicity

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** genotoxicity, tumor, chronic-toxicity

The ability of a substance to cause cancer. Assessed in 2-year rodent bioassays. Required for drugs intended for chronic use. Peptide drugs are generally considered low risk due to rapid metabolism and degradation to natural amino acids.

---

### Teratogenicity

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** developmental-toxicity, embryo, fetus

The ability of a substance to cause birth defects during fetal development. Assessed in embryo-fetal development studies in two species. Pregnancy category assigned based on animal and human data. Most peptide drugs lack teratogenic potential.

---

### Immunotoxicity

**Category:** Toxicology | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** immunogenicity, hypersensitivity, immune-suppression

Adverse effects on the immune system including immunosuppression, immunostimulation, or hypersensitivity. Peptide therapeutics may elicit anti-drug antibodies (ADAs) that reduce efficacy or cause immune-mediated adverse reactions.

---

## Formulation

### Excipient

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** formulation, preservative, stabilizer

An inactive ingredient added to a drug formulation for purposes such as stability, delivery, or patient acceptability. Common excipients for peptide drugs: mannitol (tonicity), polysorbate 80 (surfactant), histidine (buffer).

---

### Preservative

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** excipient, antimicrobial, multi-dose

An agent added to prevent microbial growth in multi-dose formulations. Examples: phenol, m-cresol, benzyl alcohol. Not required in single-use vials. Preservative-free formulations preferred for reduced injection site reactions.

---

### Surfactant

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** excipient, aggregation, stability

A surface-active agent that reduces interfacial tension. In peptide formulations, surfactants (polysorbate 20, polysorbate 80) prevent aggregation and adsorption to container surfaces. Critical for maintaining stability of liquid peptide formulations.

---

### Buffer

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** ph, excipient, stability

A solution that resists changes in pH when acid or base is added. Peptide formulations typically buffered at pH 4-7 depending on peptide stability. Common buffers: phosphate, acetate, histidine, citrate. pH affects peptide charge, solubility, and degradation rate.

---

### Lyophilization

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** freeze-drying, reconstitution, stability

Freeze-drying process that removes water from a frozen product by sublimation. Produces a stable dry powder. Common for peptide drugs to improve shelf life. Requires reconstitution before administration. Cryoprotectants (trehalose, sucrose) added to protect during freezing.

---

### Reconstitution

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** lyophilization, diluent, parenteral

The process of dissolving a lyophilized powder in a suitable diluent to prepare an injectable solution. Diluent choice affects concentration, pH, and osmolarity. Reconstituted products have limited stability and must be used within specified timeframes.

---

### Aggregation

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** fibrillation, stability, surfactant

The association of peptide molecules into higher-order species (dimers, oligomers, insoluble particles). Driven by hydrophobic interactions, concentration, temperature, and agitation. Aggregates can reduce potency and increase immunogenicity risk.

---

### Fibrillation

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** aggregation, amyloid, stability

The formation of ordered fibrillar aggregates with cross-beta structure. Some peptides (e.g., insulin, GLP-1 analogs) are prone to fibrillation under certain conditions. Fibrils can be immunogenic and are a significant formulation challenge.

---

### Stability

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** shelf-life, degradation, formulation

The ability of a drug product to maintain its chemical, physical, and microbiological properties within specified limits throughout its shelf life. Stability testing follows ICH guidelines (Q1A-Q1E). Parameters: assay, purity, pH, appearance, particulate matter.

---

### Shelf Life

**Category:** Formulation | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** stability, expiration-date, ich

The period during which a drug product is expected to remain within approved specifications when stored under recommended conditions. Determined from real-time and accelerated stability studies. Expiration date marks the end of shelf life.

---

## Clinical Trials

### Phase I Trial

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** phase-ii, safety, first-in-human

The first stage of testing in human subjects. Typically 20-100 healthy volunteers or patients. Primary objective: safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics. Determines maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and dose-limiting toxicities. Duration: several months.

---

### Phase II Trial

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** phase-i, phase-iii, efficacy

Second stage of clinical testing in a larger patient population (100-300). Evaluates therapeutic efficacy, optimal dosing, and side effects. Often randomized, controlled trials. Failure rate is high (~70%). Duration: months to 2 years.

---

### Phase III Trial

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** phase-ii, pivotal, registration

Large-scale efficacy and safety studies (300-3000+ patients) required for regulatory approval. Randomized, controlled, often multi-center and multi-national. Provides definitive evidence of benefit-risk profile. Duration: 1-4 years. Basis for NDA/BLA submission.

---

### Phase IV Trial

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** post-market, pharmacovigilance, safety-monitoring

Post-marketing studies conducted after drug approval. Monitor long-term safety, rare adverse events, drug interactions, and outcomes in special populations. May be required by regulatory agencies. Can lead to label changes or withdrawal.

---

### Informed Consent

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** ethics, irb, clinical-trial

The process of providing potential trial participants with all relevant information about a study before they agree to participate. Must include purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, and alternatives. Documented by signed consent form. Required by GCP and the Declaration of Helsinki.

---

### Randomization

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** controlled-trial, bias, stratification

The process of assigning trial participants to treatment groups by chance. Minimizes selection bias and balances known and unknown confounding factors. Methods: simple, block, stratified, adaptive. Essential for establishing causal relationships.

---

### Blinding (Masking)

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** double-blind, placebo, bias

A procedure to prevent knowledge of treatment assignment from influencing study outcomes. Single-blind: participant unaware. Double-blind: participant and investigator unaware. Triple-blind: participant, investigator, and analyst unaware. Reduces observation and reporting bias.

---

### Endpoint

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** primary-endpoint, surrogate-endpoint, outcome-measure

A precisely defined variable intended to measure an outcome of interest. Primary endpoint is the main result measured to determine efficacy. Secondary endpoints provide additional information. Choice of endpoint is critical for trial design and regulatory acceptance.

---

### Statistical Significance

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** p-value, confidence-interval, null-hypothesis

The likelihood that a result is not due to chance alone. Conventionally defined as p < 0.05. Does not imply clinical significance. Dependent on sample size and effect size. Must be interpreted alongside confidence intervals and clinical relevance.

---

### Confidence Interval

**Category:** Clinical Trials | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** p-value, statistical-significance, precision

A range of values that is likely to contain the true population parameter with a specified probability (typically 95%). Narrower intervals indicate greater precision. If 95% CI for a treatment effect does not include the null value, the result is statistically significant at p < 0.05.

---

## Regulatory

### FDA (Food and Drug Administration)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** ema, nda, ind

U.S. federal agency responsible for protecting public health by regulating drugs, biologics, medical devices, food, and cosmetics. Reviews and approves drug applications. Headquartered in Silver Spring, Maryland. Commissioner appointed by the President.

---

### EMA (European Medicines Agency)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Beginner
**Related Terms:** fda, chmp, centralized-procedure

EU regulatory agency responsible for evaluation and supervision of medicinal products. Coordinates scientific evaluation of applications through its committees. Headquartered in Amsterdam. Does not approve drugs directly; the European Commission makes final decisions.

---

### IND (Investigational New Drug Application)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** fda, nda, clinical-trial

An application submitted to FDA to begin clinical testing of a new drug in humans. Must include preclinical data, manufacturing information, and clinical protocol. FDA has 30 days to place a clinical hold if safety concerns exist.

---

### NDA (New Drug Application)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** fda, bla, drug-approval

The formal request to FDA for approval to market a new drug in the United States. Contains all preclinical and clinical data, manufacturing details, and labeling. Standard review: 10 months. Priority review: 6 months.

---

### GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** gcp, glp, quality-control

Regulations ensuring drugs are consistently produced and controlled according to quality standards. Covers facilities, equipment, personnel, documentation, and processes. Violations can result in warning letters, consent decrees, or product seizure.

---

### GCP (Good Clinical Practice)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** gmp, glp, ich

International ethical and scientific quality standard for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting clinical trials. Ensures data integrity and subject protection. Based on the Declaration of Helsinki. Compliance required by FDA, EMA, and most regulatory authorities.

---

### ICH (International Council for Harmonisation)

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** guidelines, regulatory-harmonization, tripartite

Organization that develops guidelines for pharmaceutical development to harmonize regulatory requirements across regions (US, EU, Japan). Key guidelines: Q1 (stability), Q3 (impurities), Q7 (API manufacturing), S (safety), E (efficacy).

---

### Pharmacovigilance

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** post-market-surveillance, adverse-event-reporting, safety-monitoring

The science and activities relating to detection, assessment, understanding, and prevention of adverse effects of pharmaceutical products. Encompasses pre- and post-marketing surveillance. Responsible for signal detection and risk management.

---

### Post-Market Surveillance

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** pharmacovigilance, phase-iv, risk-management

Ongoing monitoring of a drug's safety and effectiveness after regulatory approval. Includes spontaneous reporting systems (MedWatch, EudraVigilance), registries, and required post-market studies. Can identify rare or long-term adverse effects not detected in clinical trials.

---

### Adverse Event Reporting

**Category:** Regulatory | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** pharmacovigilance, medwatch, spontaneous-reporting

The mandatory reporting of adverse events to regulatory authorities. Manufacturers must report serious and unexpected events within 15 days. Healthcare professionals and consumers can submit voluntary reports. Data used for signal detection and safety assessments.

---

## Disease & Biostatistics

### Biomarker

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** surrogate-endpoint, diagnostic, companion-diagnostic

A defined characteristic measured as an indicator of normal biological processes, pathogenic processes, or pharmacological responses to therapy. Types: diagnostic, prognostic, predictive, pharmacodynamic. Can be molecules, genes, imaging features, or physiological measures.

---

### Surrogate Endpoint

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** biomarker, clinical-endpoint, accelerated-approval

A laboratory measurement or physical sign used as a substitute for a clinical endpoint. Must be reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit based on epidemiological, therapeutic, pathophysiological, or other evidence. Enables smaller, shorter trials.

---

### Clinical Endpoint

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** surrogate-endpoint, outcome, efficacy

A characteristic or variable that reflects how a patient feels, functions, or survives. Direct measure of clinical benefit. Examples: overall survival, progression-free survival, symptom severity. Gold standard for demonstrating efficacy but requires longer follow-up.

---

### Hazard Ratio

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** survival-analysis, confidence-interval, cox-regression

The ratio of hazard rates between two groups at any given time. HR = 1 indicates no difference. HR < 1 favors treatment. HR > 1 favors control. Derived from Cox proportional hazards model. Reported with 95% confidence interval and p-value.

---

### Odds Ratio

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** relative-risk, case-control, cross-sectional

The ratio of odds of an event occurring in the treatment group to the odds in the control group. Used in case-control studies where incidence cannot be calculated. OR ≈ 1 indicates no association. Interpretation differs from relative risk when events are common.

---

### Relative Risk

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** odds-ratio, risk-ratio, cohort-study

The ratio of the probability of an event in the treatment group to the probability in the control group. RR = 1 indicates no difference. RR < 1 indicates reduced risk. RR > 1 indicates increased risk. Used in cohort studies and randomized controlled trials.

---

### Meta-Analysis

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** systematic-review, forest-plot, heterogeneity

A statistical method that combines results from multiple independent studies to produce a single estimate of effect. Increases statistical power and precision. Requires assessment of heterogeneity (I² statistic). Results displayed in forest plots.

---

### Systematic Review

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** meta-analysis, prisma, evidence-synthesis

A comprehensive, structured review of all relevant studies on a specific question using predefined methods. Follows PRISMA guidelines for reporting. Includes systematic search, quality assessment, and evidence synthesis. Foundation for evidence-based guidelines.

---

### Evidence-Based Medicine

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Related Terms:** systematic-review, clinical-guideline, hierarchy-of-evidence

The integration of best research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values. Hierarchy: systematic reviews > RCTs > cohort studies > case-control > case reports > expert opinion. Clinical practice guidelines are developed from the best available evidence.

---

### Odds Ratio (vs. Relative Risk)

**Category:** Disease | **Difficulty:** Advanced
**Related Terms:** relative-risk, epidemiology, study-design

A measure of association commonly used in case-control studies. When the outcome is rare (<10%), odds ratio approximates relative risk. When the outcome is common, odds ratio overestimates relative risk. Important for interpreting meta-analyses that combine different study designs.
