## Structure

C-peptide is a 31-amino acid peptide that connects the A and B chains of proinsulin in the precursor molecule. During insulin biosynthesis, proinsulin is cleaved by prohormone convertases PC1/3 and PC2, releasing C-peptide and mature insulin in equimolar amounts.

## Mechanism of Action

C-peptide was long considered biologically inactive, but evidence suggests it binds to a G-protein-coupled receptor (possibly CXCR4) on cell surfaces, activating eNOS and Na+/K+-ATPase. It may have beneficial effects on microvascular blood flow, nerve function, and renal function in type 1 diabetes.

## Clinical Applications

- **Beta-cell function assessment**: C-peptide levels reflect endogenous insulin production
- **Type 1 vs type 2 diabetes**: Distinguishes insulin deficiency from resistance
- **Hypoglycemia evaluation**: Factitious vs insulinoma
- **Research**: Potential therapeutic in type 1 diabetes

## Pharmacology

- **Half-life**: 20-30 minutes (vs 3-5 min for insulin)
- **Molar ratio**: 1:1 with insulin
- **Renal clearance**: 5% of insulin clearance
- **Stability**: More stable than insulin in blood samples
